Understanding Key Terms

British India had three main administrative units — the Three Presidencies: Bengal, Bombay, and Madras.

Bengal was the most important presidency, housing 40% of British India's population.

Governor of Bengal

The initial administrative head of Bengal. First Governor of Bengal: Robert Clive (served twice).

Governor General of Bengal

  • Created by the Regulating Act of 1773 to control corruption within the East India Company.
  • The Governor of Bengal was elevated to Governor General of Bengal.
  • Governors of Bombay and Madras were made subordinate to the Governor General of Bengal.
  • First Governor General of Bengal: Warren Hastings (also the last Governor of Bengal).

Governor General of India

  • Created by the Charter Act of 1833 (also known as the St. Helena Act).
  • The Governor General of Bengal was elevated to Governor General of India.
  • First Governor General of India: William Bentinck (also the last Governor General of Bengal).

Viceroy

  • After the Revolt of 1857, the East India Company's rule ended, and Crown Rule was established by the Government of India Act of 1858.
  • The title of Governor General of India was changed to Viceroy.

📖 Why were these posts created? (tap to expand)
The Regulating Act of 1773 created the Governor General of Bengal specifically to control corruption within the East India Company, and made the Governors of Bombay and Madras subordinate to this new post — bringing all three Presidencies under one central authority for the first time.

Warren Hastings (1773–1785)

First Governor General of Bengal.

Key Acts

  • Regulating Act of 1773.
  • Pitt's India Act of 1784.

Wars

  • First Anglo-Maratha War — ended with the Treaty of Salbai.
  • Second Anglo-Mysore War — ended with the Treaty of Mangalore. Hyder Ali was the ruler of Mysore during this war.

Other

Establishment of the Asiatic Society of Bengal in 1784 by William Jones. Objective: translate Indian literature into English.

Lord Cornwallis (1786–1793)

Key Reforms

  • Introduced the Permanent Settlement (also known as the Zamindari System) in 1793 in the Bengal region.
  • Known as the Father of Indian Civil Services — introduced a merit-based civil services system.
  • Introduced the Cornwallis Code, which brought judicial reforms and separated revenue administration from civil jurisdiction.

Wars

  • Third Anglo-Mysore War — ended with the Treaty of Seringapatam.

Other

Died in India; his tomb is in Ghazipur.

🎯 Exam Tip Cornwallis = 3 C's: Civil services (merit-based), Cornwallis Code (judicial reform), and Permanent Settlement (Zamindari, Bengal, 1793).
❌ Common Mistake Don't confuse Cornwallis's Permanent Settlement (Zamindari, Bengal, 1793) with the Ryotwari or Mahalwari systems introduced later by other Governors-General.
🧠 Memory Trick Cornwallis died in India and is buried in Ghazipur — he stayed "permanently" in India, just like his Permanent Settlement was meant to last forever!

Lord Wellesley (1798–1805)

Key Policy — Subsidiary Alliance (1798)

  • Objective: prevent French (Napoleonic) influence by making Indian rulers dependent on the British.
  • Provisions: stationing a British Resident, disbanding native armies, maintaining British forces — in exchange for protection.
  • First to adopt: Nizam of Hyderabad (1798).
  • Other adopters: Mysore (1799), Awadh (1801).

Lord Hastings (1813–1823)

Wars

  • Third Anglo-Maratha War (1817–1819).
  • Anglo-Nepal War — ended with the Treaty of Sugauli.

Land Revenue Systems

  • Ryotwari System — introduced in 1820 in Madras and other regions. Introduced by Munro and Read. Direct revenue collection from peasants (Ryots).
  • Mahalwari System — introduced in 1822 in Punjab and NWFP regions. Introduced by Holt Mackenzie and William Bentinck. Revenue collected from groups of villages (Mahals).

William Bentinck (1828–1835)

First Governor General of India.

Social Reforms

  • Abolition of Sati (with efforts from Raja Ram Mohan Roy).
  • Suppression of the Thuggee system.

Administrative Reforms

  • Abolished Provincial and Circuit Courts (introduced by Cornwallis).

Education

  • Macaulay's Minute (1835) introduced, promoting English education — based on the Downward Filtration Theory.
  • Sometimes called the Father of English Education in India.

Lord Dalhousie (1848–1856)

Key Policy (Negative) — Doctrine of Lapse

Introduced in 1848. Policy: if an Indian ruler died without a natural heir, their kingdom would be annexed by the British.

Summary

The British administration in India evolved through different phases, marked by changes in the title and powers of its chief representative. Initially, Governors managed individual presidencies. The Regulating Act of 1773 centralized power by creating the Governor General of Bengal, making Warren Hastings the first to hold this expanded role. The Charter Act of 1833 further consolidated authority, establishing the Governor General of India, with William Bentinck as the first. Finally, the Government of India Act of 1858, following the 1857 Revolt, replaced the Governor General with the Viceroy, signifying direct Crown rule.

1773 — Regulating Act — created Governor General of Bengal (first: Warren Hastings)
1833 — Charter Act — created Governor General of India (first: William Bentinck)
1858 — Government of India Act — replaced Governor General with Viceroy; Crown Rule begins
1773 → 1833 → 1858: Bengal → India → Viceroy
