Muhammad bin Qasim — The First Islamic Invader

Our medieval history begins with the Islamic invasion of India. The very first Islamic invader was Muhammad bin Qasim.

🗺️ Fig 1: Bin Qasim's Invasion Trail

Where From & Whom Did He Defeat?

  • Muhammad bin Qasim was an Arab invader — he came from Arabia
  • He invaded the Sindh province, located in present-day Pakistan
  • The ruler of Sindh, Raja Dahir (King Dahir), was defeated and killed by him
❌ Common Mistake Muhammad bin Qasim is not a "Turk" invader — he was an Arab, from Arabia! The first Turk invader was Mahmud of Ghaznavi — coming up next!
🧠 Memory TrickThree "firsts" for Bin Qasim: 1st Islamic invader of India, 1st to attack Sindh, 1st to introduce the Jizya!

Jizya — Introduced for the First Time

It is believed that Muhammad bin Qasim introduced the Jizya for the first time — the tax paid by non-Muslims.

🎯 Exam Tip Jizya = tax on non-Muslims. Remember: Bin Qasim is credited as the FIRST to introduce it in India.
📖 His End — the story of Dahir's daughters (tap to expand)
It is said Muhammad bin Qasim took two daughters of King Dahir and sent them to the Sultan of Arabia. The girls told the Sultan that Muhammad bin Qasim had exploited them before sending them on — so the Sultan had him hanged and executed.

This backstory is not important from an exam perspective — only the 3 key facts above (first invader, killed Raja Dahir, introduced Jizya) are actually tested.
⚔️ Legend has it: Raja Dahir's own daughters brought down his conqueror — accused before the Sultan of Arabia, Muhammad bin Qasim was hanged and executed!

Quick Recap Chain

Arabia→ Sindh invaded→ Raja Dahir killed→ Jizya introduced→ Hanged & executed

Mahmud of Ghaznavi — The First Turk Invader

The second Islamic invader — and the first Turk invader — was Mahmud of Ghaznavi.

❌ Common Mistake "Turk" here does not mean someone from Turkey — it means someone from Turkmenistan (Central Asia)!

17 Attacks on India

Mahmud of Ghaznavi attacked India a total of 17 times. Of these, the 1st, 16th, and 17th attacks matter most for exams.

🧠 Fig 2: The 17 Attacks — Mind Map

1st Attack — Battle of Peshawar

  • His first attack took place in 1000 AD
  • It is known as the Battle of Peshawar
  • Fought against King Jaipal — Jaipal was very badly defeated
  • It is believed Jaipal committed suicide after this defeat
  • Fun fact: Peshawar was formerly known as Purushpura
📌 Fun FactPeshawar — the site of Ghaznavi's very first attack — was earlier called Purushpura!

16th & 17th Attacks — Somnath & the Jats

  • 16th attack: on the Somnath Temple, in 1025 AD — he looted and plundered the temple's wealth
  • 17th (and last) attack: in 1027 AD, against the Jats — revenge, since the Jats troubled him while he carried the looted Somnath wealth back
  • Main motive throughout: plunder (to fund and expand his empire back home), not settling down to rule India
💡 Understand it like this... Ghaznavi's raids were smash-and-grab missions, not conquest-and-settle. He looted wealth — like Somnath's treasures — to expand his empire on the Arab/Central-Asian side, never to rule India directly!
🧠 Memory Trick 1st fought a KING (Jaipal, Peshawar) → 16th looted a TEMPLE (Somnath) → 17th chased the JATS (revenge) — 1-King, 16-Temple, 17-Jats!

📅 Timeline of the Key Attacks

1000 AD
Battle of Peshawar — defeats King Jaipal (his 1st attack).
1025 AD
16th attack — loots the Somnath Temple.
1027 AD
17th & last attack — against the Jats, in revenge.
🗡️ 17 TOTAL invasions — but only these 3 matter most for exams!
🎯 Exam Tip Order matters: 1st (Peshawar, Jaipal) → 16th (Somnath) → 17th (Jats, last). Don't swap 16th and 17th!

Ghaznavi's Wars, Books & Death

Battles of Waihind — vs the Hindu Shahi Dynasty

Mahmud of Ghaznavi fought a series of wars against the Hindu Shahi Dynasty — together known as the Battles of Waihind. (King Jaipal, defeated at Peshawar, belonged to this very Hindu Shahi Dynasty.)

🗺️ Fig 3: Ghaznavi's Rivals & Contemporaries

Contemporary Rulers

Mahmud of Ghaznavi was a contemporary of the Chola Dynasty rulers of South India — specifically Rajendra Chola I.

📖 Hindu Shahi Dynasty & Waihind — background (tap to expand)
The Hindu Shahi kings ruled the north-western frontier of the subcontinent, around Peshawar/Waihind. King Jaipal, defeated in the very first attack (Battle of Peshawar, 1000 AD), was a Hindu Shahi ruler. Ghaznavi's later campaigns against this dynasty are collectively remembered as the Battles of Waihind.

Books Written During His Time

Two important books were written during Mahmud of Ghaznavi's era — not written by him, but by scholars of his time.

TextAuthorNote
ShahnamaFerdowsiPersian epic — Ghaznavi's era
Tahqiq-e-Hind (Kitab-ul-Hind)Al-BiruniStudy of India — Ghaznavi's era
BadshahnamaAbdul Hamid Lahori❌ NOT this era — later Mughal text!
❌ Common Mistake Don't confuse Shahnama (Ferdowsi, Ghaznavi's era) with Badshahnama (Abdul Hamid Lahori, a much later Mughal-era book)! Similar-sounding names, completely different books.
📖 Two courts, two books: Ferdowsi's Shahnama immortalized Persia's kings; Al-Biruni's Kitab-ul-Hind studied India itself — both flourished in Ghaznavi's time.
🎯 Exam Tip Tahqiq-e-Hind = Kitab-ul-Hind = the same book, by Al-Biruni. Two names, one author!

Death

Mahmud of Ghaznavi died in 1030 AD.

Muhammad Ghori — The 3rd Islamic Invader

The third Islamic invader — also called the second Turk invader — was Muhammad Ghori. This is where the foundation of the Delhi Sultanate begins — very important for exams!

Early Campaigns — Multan & Gujarat

YearPlaceResult
1175 ADMultanFirst attack — captured
1178 ADGujaratDefeated by Naiki Devi!
  • 1175 AD — Ghori's first attack, on the Multan region (in present-day Pakistan)
  • 1178 AD — attacked Gujarat, then ruled by Mularaja II and Bhima II, whose mother (and regent) was Naiki Devi
  • Ghori was defeated in Gujarat itself, by Naiki Devi
🧠 Memory TrickA QUEEN stopped Ghori before Prithviraj ever fought him — Naiki Devi, Gujarat, 1178!
📖 Delhi before the Chauhans — the Tomar Dynasty (tap to expand)
Before the Chauhan dynasty ruled Delhi, it was ruled by the Tomar Dynasty, founded by Anangpal Tomar. Delhi was the Tomars' first capital. The Chauhans took over after them — and it was a Chauhan king, Prithviraj III, who fought Ghori at Tarain.

Battles of Tarain (near Delhi)

Tarain lies in the Terai region near Delhi — the same "Terai/Tarai plains" you'll meet again in geography, and later with Babur's battles!

There were two Battles of Tarain, fought between Muhammad Ghori and Prithviraj III (Prithviraj Chauhan):

⚔️ Fig 4: Tarain I → Tarain II — The Reversal
Multan '75 ✓→ Gujarat '78 ✗→ Tarain I '91 ✗→ Tarain II '92 ✓
🎯 Exam Tip Tarain I (1191) = Prithviraj wins. Tarain II (1192) = Ghori wins. The SECOND battle changed everything!
🧠 Memory TrickBefore Chauhans: TOMAR dynasty ruled Delhi (founder: Anangpal Tomar). Chauhans came next, and lost Delhi to Ghori in Tarain II (1192).

Chandawar, the Slaves & the Sultanate

Battle of Chandawar (1194 AD)

After Tarain, Ghori fought and defeated Jaichand at the Battle of Chandawar in 1194 AD. Jaichand ruled Chandawar (Kannauj), and belonged to the Gahadavala Dynasty.

📖 Sanyogita, Prithviraj Raso & the Jaichand legend (tap to expand)
Jaichand's daughter was Sanyogita, whom Prithviraj III wished to marry. This story is found in the Prithviraj Raso, written by Chandrabardai (also spelled Chand Bardai) — though some historians dispute whether this actually happened. Legend says a jilted Jaichand then invited Ghori to defeat Prithviraj Chauhan — but Ghori went on to defeat Jaichand too, at Chandawar!
👑 Jaichand invited Ghori to defeat his rival Prithviraj — but the invader Ghori turned around and defeated Jaichand as well!
❌ Common Mistake The Sanyogita–Prithviraj romance comes from the Prithviraj Raso — some historians doubt its historical accuracy. Don't present it as an undisputed fact in your answers!

Ghori's Return & His Four Slaves

After Chandawar, Ghori returned home. His most important legacy in India came through his four slaves:

❌ Common Mistake Bakhtiyar Khilji (Ghori's slave, destroyed Nalanda) is not the same person as Alauddin Khilji (a much later, unrelated Delhi Sultan)! Similar surname, different centuries.

Nalanda & Vikramshila — Universities Lost & Built

  • Bakhtiyar Khilji is remembered for destroying Nalanda University
  • Nalanda was built by Kumar Gupta (of the Gupta Dynasty)
  • Vikramshila (another great university) was built by rulers of the Pala Dynasty
📌 Fun FactNalanda (Gupta-built) and Vikramshila (Pala-built) were two of ancient India's greatest universities. Bakhtiyar Khilji's raid on Nalanda was a huge loss for Indian learning.

Qutub-ud-din Aibak Founds the Delhi Sultanate

Of Ghori's four slaves, Qutub-ud-din Aibak was the most loyal and capable. He ascended the throne of Delhi and began to rule — laying the foundation of the Delhi Sultanate.
