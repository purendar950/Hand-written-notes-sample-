PHYSICS — ELECTRIC POWER
Q1.
A 50W bulb is connected to a 100V battery. If the resistance of the bulb is doubled, what will be the power consumed?
✔ Answer: 25 Watt (power gets halved)
  • Power formulas: P = V²/R = VI = I²R — use whichever variable is constant.
  • Here V is constant ⇒ use P = V²/R ⇒ P ∝ 1/R (inverse, single power).
  • Resistance doubled ⇒ Power becomes half ⇒ 50W → 25W.
⚠ Don't confuse with KE = ½mv² (v² term, so doubling v ⇒ 4×). Here it's a single power (R¹), so relation is simple inverse, not inverse-square.
PHYSICS — HEATING & MAGNETIC EFFECT OF CURRENT
Q2.
Who discovered the heating effect and the magnetic effect of electric current?
✔ Heating effect: James Prescott Joule   |   Magnetic effect: Hans Christian Ørsted (1820)
  • Heating effect formula: H = I²Rt (Joule's Law of Heating).
  • Electric current has BOTH a heating (thermal) effect and a magnetic effect.
  • Ørsted's 1820 experiment (compass needle deflecting near a current-carrying wire) is the founding observation of electromagnetism.
PHYSICS — ACCELERATED CHARGED PARTICLES
Q3.
A proton and an alpha particle are accelerated through the same potential difference. What is the ratio of their final velocities?
✔ Answer: √2 : 1
  • Work done = KE ⇒ qV = ½mv² ⇒ v = √(2qV/m)
  • Alpha particle: charge = 2e (double proton's charge), mass = 4m (≈4× proton's mass).
  • v_p²/v_α² = (q_p/q_α) × (m_α/m_p) = (1/2) × 4 = 2 ⇒ v_p/v_α = √2
📌 Exam Tip: Same potential difference + different q,m ⇒ always reduce to v ∝ √(q/m) and plug ratios directly.

PHYSICS — PLANE MIRRORS (MULTIPLE IMAGES)
Q4.
If two plane mirrors are inclined at 60° to each other, how many images of an object will be formed?
✔ Answer: 5 images
  • Let n = 360°/θ. Here n = 360/60 = 6 (an integer, and even).
  • Rule: if n is even → images = n − 1 (regardless of object position).
  • If n is odd: images = n−1 (object on the bisector / symmetric) or n (object off the bisector / asymmetric).
  • So here: 6 − 1 = 5 images.
Fig 1 — Two mirrors at 60°, image count = n−1
🧠 Memory Trick: 90° mirrors → n=4(even)→3 images. 60°→5. Special case: mirrors parallel (0°/360°) → infinite images!

CHEMISTRY — CARBON ALLOTROPES (FULLERENE)
Q5.
How many hexagonal and pentagonal rings are present in Buckminsterfullerene, C₆₀?
✔ Answer: 20 hexagons + 12 pentagons = football/soccer-ball shape
  • Diamond: each C bonded to 4 C's → sp³ → hardest natural substance; refractive index 2.42; shows Total Internal Reflection.
  • Graphite: each C bonded to 3 C's → sp² → slippery (used as lubricant), good conductor of electricity; layers held by weak van der Waals forces.
  • Graphene: single 2D layer, extremely thin, flexible, stronger than steel, good conductor.
  • Fullerene (C₆₀): 60 carbon atoms in a hollow ball shape — 20 hexagonal + 12 pentagonal rings.

PHYSICS — OPTICS (AIR BUBBLE IN WATER)
Q8.
How does an air bubble inside water behave with respect to light rays?
✔ Answer: Behaves like a Concave (diverging) lens
  • Air bubble = lower refractive-index medium surrounded by higher refractive-index medium (water) ⇒ diverges light rays.
  • Also shows Total Internal Reflection at some angles → bubble appears shiny, mirror-like.
⚠ Common Mistake: Don't assume bubble converges light like a convex lens — the surrounding denser medium makes it behave oppositely (diverging).
CHEMISTRY — DISPLACEMENT REACTION
Q9.
An iron nail is dipped in a blue aqueous solution of copper sulfate. The solution gradually turns light green. This is an example of which reaction?
✔ Answer: Displacement reaction (also a redox reaction)
  • Iron is more reactive than copper (higher in reactivity series) → displaces Cu from CuSO₄.
  • Reaction: Fe + CuSO₄ → FeSO₄ + Cu
  • Blue CuSO₄ solution turns light green (FeSO₄, not colourless).
  • Iron nail gets a reddish-brown deposit of copper on it.
📌 Related decomposition facts: Lead nitrate decomposition → brown fumes of NO₂ gas. Silver bromide decomposition (thermal) → used in black & white photography.

CHEMISTRY — OXIDATION STATES
Q10.
What is the oxidation state of Chromium in Potassium Dichromate (K₂Cr₂O₇)?
✔ Answer: +6
  • K = +1 (×2 = +2), O = −2 (×7 = −14). Let Cr = x (×2).
  • 2 + 2x − 14 = 0 ⇒ 2x = 12 ⇒ x = +6
  • Bonus: Oxidation state of Mn in KMnO₄ (Potassium Permanganate) = +7 (1 + x − 8 = 0 ⇒ x = 7).
PHYSICS — FORCE ON CURRENT-CARRYING CONDUCTOR
Q11.
Under what condition is the force on a current-carrying conductor in a uniform magnetic field maximum?
✔ Answer: When conductor is perpendicular to the magnetic field (θ = 90°)
  • Formula: F = BIL sinθ — max when sinθ = 1 i.e. θ = 90°.
  • Right-hand thumb rule (or Maxwell's screw rule): thumb = current direction, curled fingers = magnetic field direction around the wire.
  • Field strength depends on: (1) magnitude of current, (2) distance from the conductor (inversely).
  • Fleming's Left-Hand Rule → force direction on current-carrying conductor (Motor effect).
  • Fleming's Right-Hand Rule → induced current direction (Generator/Dynamo effect).

BIOLOGY — PROTOZOA NUTRITION
Q12.
Which cellular structure helps Amoeba obtain its food?
✔ Answer: Plasma membrane — forms pseudopodia
  • Pseudopodia ("false feet") — plasma membrane extensions that surround food particles, forming a food vacuole.
  • Amoeba has no fixed shape; nutrition mode = Holozoic (ingest → digest → egest, same as humans).
  • Cilia (hair-like) → Paramecium (slipper-shaped, uses cilia to move).
  • Flagella (whip-like) → Euglena.
PHYSICS — CONVEX LENS
Q13.
Parallel rays (parallel to principal axis) fall on a convex lens. Where do the refracted rays go?
✔ Answer: They converge at the principal focus on the other side
  • Convex lens ≡ Concave mirror (same object-image position logic).
  • Object at infinity → image at focus: real, inverted, highly diminished (point-sized).
PHYSICS — WAVE EQUATION
Q14.
Wavelength of light = 600 nm, speed = 3×10⁸ m/s. Find its frequency.
✔ Answer: 5 × 10¹⁴ Hz
  • v = nλ ⇒ n = v/λ = (3×10⁸) / (600×10⁻⁹) = (3×10⁸)/(6×10⁻⁷) = 5×10¹⁴ Hz
  • Frequency doesn't change with medium — only depends on the source.
  • Pitch of sound depends on frequency; loudness depends on amplitude. Human hearing range: 20 Hz – 20 kHz.

BIOLOGY — CELL ORGANELLES (CHLOROPLAST)
Q15.
Which cell organelle in a leaf contains the chlorophyll needed for photosynthesis?
✔ Answer: Chloroplast
  • Chlorophyll's central metal atom = Magnesium. Chlorophyll a/b absorb red & blue light, reflect green → leaves look green.
  • Thylakoids (stacked as grana) → site of Light reaction. Stroma → site of Dark reaction (Calvin cycle / light-independent reaction).
  • Plastids: Chloroplast (green, photosynthesis), Chromoplast (colour), Leucoplast (storage: Amyloplast–carbs, Aleuroplast–protein, Elaioplast–fats).
  • Vacuole (not the answer here): single large vacuole in plant cells, 50–90% of cell volume, gives turgidity.
  • Mitochondria (not the answer here): powerhouse of cell, produces ATP at cristae (inner membrane folds), sausage-shaped, double membrane.
  • Nucleus: discovered by Robert Brown (1831); contains chromosomes (DNA + protein).
CHEMISTRY — CHEMICAL vs PHYSICAL CHANGE
Q16.
Iron filings mixed with sulfur powder and heated form a black substance, Iron Sulfide. What kind of change is this?
✔ Answer: Chemical change (irreversible)
  • New substance formed (FeS) — not a mixture/solution, hence chemical, not physical.
  • Physical change = only state changes, reversible (e.g. ice ↔ water); Chemical change = new substance, generally irreversible (e.g. milk → curd).

PHYSICS — OHM'S LAW
Q19.
If a conductor has a potential difference of 1V across it and current of 1A flows through it, what is its resistance?
✔ Answer: 1 Ohm (Ω)
  • Ohm's Law: V = IR ⇒ R = V/I = 1/1 = 1Ω
  • Resistance R = ρL/A — depends on: length (∝), area (∝ 1/A), nature of material (ρ), and temperature (∝).
  • ρ (specific resistance/resistivity) unit = ohm-metre (Ωm).
CHEMISTRY — MOLE CONCEPT
Q20.
Molecular mass of a compound is 63u. It contains 1 hydrogen atom and 3 oxygen atoms. What is the atomic mass of the remaining element?
✔ Answer: 14u (Nitrogen)
  • 63 = 1(H) + 3×16(O) + x ⇒ 63 = 1 + 48 + x ⇒ x = 14
  • Compound = HNO₃ (Nitric acid), also called Aqua Fortis.
  • Related: HCl = Muriatic acid; H₂SO₄ = "King of Chemicals" / Oil of Vitriol (used in lead-acid batteries).
CHEMISTRY — PERIODIC TRENDS
Q21.
Which factor primarily determines the size of an atom?
✔ Answer: Number of electron shells
  • Down a group: shells increase (K, L, M, N…) → size increases. This is the primary factor.
  • Across a period: nuclear charge increases → pulls electrons closer → size decreases. This is a secondary factor (no new shell added).

Due to biological magnification, the maximum concentration of pesticides is found at which trophic level?
✔ Answer: Tertiary consumer (top of food chain)
  • Bioaccumulation: toxin concentration increases within a single organism/trophic level over time.
  • Biomagnification: toxin transfers & concentrates as it moves up the food chain (e.g. DDT).

CHEMISTRY — ELECTROLYTIC REFINING
Q24.
What are the essential properties of the electrolyte used in electrolytic refining of metals?
✔ Answer: An aqueous solution of a suitable soluble salt of the metal being refined
  • Anode (positive terminal) = impure metal. Cathode (negative terminal) = thin strip, where pure metal deposits.
  • Impurities settle below the anode as anode mud/sludge.
  • Used for metals of high reactivity (medium/low reactivity metals are purified by reduction of their oxide instead).
PHYSICS — ARCHIMEDES' PRINCIPLE
Q25.
Which of the following devices works on Archimedes' Principle?
(a) Sphygmomanometer   (b) Altimeter   (c) Hydrometer   (d) Aneroid barometer
✔ Answer: (c) Hydrometer (measures liquid density)
  • Archimedes' Principle: buoyant force on a submerged body = weight of fluid displaced.
  • Sphygmomanometer → blood pressure. Altimeter → height. Aneroid barometer → atmospheric pressure (mechanical, not buoyancy).
  • Lactometer (measures milk purity) also works on Archimedes' Principle.
📌 Ship floats but a nail sinks: ship's shape displaces a much larger volume of water, so buoyant force balances its weight; the nail can't displace enough.

PHYSICS — NEWTON'S LAWS OF MOTION
Q27.
When a person stands stationary, is the gravitational force (Earth on person) and the normal force (ground on person) an action-reaction pair under Newton's Third Law?
✔ Answer: No — they're equal & opposite, but act on the SAME body (balanced forces), not an action-reaction pair
  • True action-reaction pairs act on two different bodies: (Earth pulls person down) ↔ (person pulls Earth up); (ground pushes person up) ↔ (person pushes ground down).
  • Gravity + Normal force here act on the same body (the person) and cancel out → net force = 0 → no motion.
  • Newton's 1st Law (Inertia): body stays at rest/uniform motion unless an external unbalanced force acts (bus stopping → passenger thrown forward).
  • Newton's 2nd Law: F = rate of change of momentum = ma (since p = mv, and a = dv/dt).
  • Newton's 3rd Law: every action has an equal and opposite reaction, on two different bodies.
⚠ Common Mistake: Don't call any pair of "equal and opposite" forces an action-reaction pair — check they act on different objects!

BIOLOGY — HUMAN REPRODUCTIVE SYSTEM
Q28.
In which part of the female reproductive system does fertilization normally occur?
✔ Answer: Fallopian tube (oviduct)
  • Ovaries release the egg (ovum, female gamete) → travels toward fallopian tube.
  • Sperm (male gamete) enters via vagina → cervix → uterus → fallopian tube.
  • Egg + Sperm fuse in the fallopian tube → forms the Zygote.
  • Order of development: Zygote → Embryo → Fetus (fetus stage from ~end of 2nd month; all major organs visible by 9th month).
  • Zygote implants in uterus wall; Placenta transfers nutrients (mother→baby) and waste (baby→mother).
  • If fertilization doesn't occur, thickened uterine lining sheds as blood → Menstruation.

What is common between chloroplasts and mitochondria?

CHEMISTRY — ORGANIC REACTIONS
Q31.
What happens in a substitution reaction?
✔ Answer: One atom/group is replaced by another atom/group
  • Example: CH₄ + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + HCl (one H replaced by Cl).
  • Contrast — Addition reaction: atoms add across a double/triple bond, e.g. Ethene + H₂ →(Ni/Pt catalyst) Ethane.

CHEMISTRY — SALTS & pH
Q34.
Which salt will give a solution with pH close to 7 when dissolved in water?
✔ Answer: NaCl (Sodium Chloride)
  • Strong acid + Strong base → neutral salt (pH ≈ 7): HCl + NaOH → NaCl.
  • Weak acid + Strong base → basic salt (e.g. CH₃COONa). Strong acid + Weak base → acidic salt (e.g. NH₄Cl).

PHYSICS — ATMOSPHERIC REFRACTION
Q35.
The wavering/shimmering appearance of objects seen through hot air rising above a fire is due to which phenomenon?
✔ Answer: Atmospheric refraction
  • Hot air layers have varying density → varying refractive index → light bends continuously.
  • Same phenomenon causes: Mirage, Twinkling of stars, and early sunrise/delayed sunset (~2 min each).
  • Scattering/Dispersion (Rayleigh) → explains sky's blue colour, red sun at sunrise/sunset.
  • Tyndall effect: scattering of light by colloidal particles/dust (described by John Tyndall, 1869) — shown by heterogeneous mixtures, NOT true (homogeneous) solutions.
  • Diffraction = bending of light around obstacles/edges.
PHYSICS — MOTION GRAPHS
Q36.
If the distance-time graph of an object is a straight line parallel to the time axis, what is its speed?
✔ Answer: Zero
  • Distance stays constant as time passes → object not moving → speed = 0.
  • Distance-time graph → gives speed. Velocity-time graph → gives acceleration.
  • Distance = scalar; Displacement = vector (shortest path, initial→final position). Displacement ≤ Distance always.
PHYSICS — GRAVITATIONAL PE & WORK
Q37.
How is the change in gravitational potential energy related to the work done by the gravitational force?
✔ Answer: Work done by gravity = − (Change in PE)
  • Work done against gravity (lifting up) = +ΔPE (PE increases).
  • Work done by gravity (falling down) = −ΔPE (PE decreases, converts to KE).

BIOLOGY — GENETICS & VARIATION
Q39.
Why do children of the same parents resemble each other but are not exactly identical?
✔ Answer: They inherit different combinations of genetic variations from their parents
  • Each gene has two alleles — Gregor Mendel ("Father of Genetics") gave 3 laws using Pea plant (Pisum sativum): Law of Dominance, Law of Segregation, Law of Independent Assortment.
  • DNA molecule first discovered by Friedrich Miescher; double-helix structure given by Watson & Crick.
  • Base pairing: Adenine–Thymine, Guanine–Cytosine. In RNA, Uracil replaces Thymine.
  • DNA replication is semi-conservative (one old strand + one new strand in each copy) — errors/mutations are rare, but are the main source of variation between siblings.

PHYSICS — CURRENT ELECTRICITY (RECAP)
Q42.
According to Ohm's Law, the resistance of a conductor depends on what?
✔ Answer: Nature of the conductor and its physical state (e.g. temperature) — NOT on current or potential difference
  • R = ρL/A: length (direct), area (inverse), nature of material (ρ), temperature (direct — resistance ↑ with temperature).
  • Copper is costlier than aluminium because it has higher conductivity (lower resistivity).
PHYSICS — ELECTROSTATICS
Q43.
(a) What is the SI unit of capacitance? (b) In Coulomb's Law, if the distance between two charges is doubled, what happens to the force?
✔ (a) Farad (F)   (b) Force becomes 1/4th
  • SI unit of charge = Coulomb.
  • Coulomb's Law: F = kq₁q₂/r² — inverse-square law.
  • r → 2r ⇒ F → F/4 (since r² term in denominator).

BIOLOGY — RESPIRATION
Q45.
When oxygen availability decreases, which stage of aerobic respiration is affected first?
✔ Answer: Krebs cycle / Electron Transport Chain (both directly need O₂; Glycolysis does not)
  • Glycolysis: glucose (6C) → pyruvate (3C), in cytoplasm, doesn't directly require O₂ → 2 ATP + 2 NADH.
  • Krebs cycle (Citric acid cycle): in mitochondrial matrix.
  • Electron Transport Chain: in inner mitochondrial membrane (cristae) — produces most ATP; O₂ is the final electron acceptor here, so this is hit hardest when O₂ falls.
  • Low O₂ in muscles → anaerobic pathway → lactic acid build-up → cramps/fatigue.
BIOLOGY — EXCRETION
Q46.
How is metabolic waste removed in unicellular organisms?
✔ Answer: By simple diffusion across the cell membrane
  • High surface-area-to-volume ratio in unicellular organisms makes diffusion sufficient — no specialised excretory organs needed.
  • (In humans, filtration happens via the nephron: Glomerulus → PCT → Loop of Henle → DCT → Collecting duct.)
