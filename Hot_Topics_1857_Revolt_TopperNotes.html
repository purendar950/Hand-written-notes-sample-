① Background — Governor-General & Key Policies 🏛️

Setting the Stage:

  • Lord Canning was the Governor-General when the 1857 Revolt broke out
  • Revolt of 1857 is also called the "Sepoy Mutiny" — because it was primarily soldiers (sepoys) who revolted
Q1. Which Act, introduced under Canning, required Bengal Army soldiers to serve overseas — violating Hindu religious beliefs about crossing the sea?
A
General Service Enlistment Act ✔
✅ Answer: General Service Enlistment Act — Bengal Army soldiers (mostly Hindu) were told they might need to serve overseas. Crossing the sea ("Kala Pani") was believed to destroy one's religion at the time — this fueled resentment among sepoys
✦ ✦ ✦
② Doctrine of Lapse — Dalhousie's Annexation Policy 👑
Q2. Who introduced the Doctrine of Lapse, and in which year?
A
Lord Dalhousie, 1848 ✔
✅ Answer: Lord Dalhousie introduced the Doctrine of Lapse in 1848

Doctrine of Lapse — Core Rule:

  • If a ruler died without a "natural" (biological) heir, the British would NOT recognize an adopted son's right to the throne
  • The British reserved full discretion on whether to grant succession rights at all
  • If rights were denied → the kingdom would be annexed directly by the British
🧠 British Strategy — "Small loan trick": Just like a con artist first builds trust with small favors before taking a big one and disappearing, the British first offered princely states protection via Subsidiary Alliance (no problems initially), then later used the Doctrine of Lapse to annex them outright.
Doctrine of Lapse — States Annexed (Chronological)
1848 Satara — first state annexed under Doctrine of Lapse
1849 Sambalpur & Jaitpur
1852 Udaipur
1853 Jhansi
1856 Awadh — annexed on grounds of "misgovernance" (NOT Doctrine of Lapse directly)
✦ ✦ ✦
③ Annexation of Awadh — Nawab Wajid Ali Shah 🕌
Q3. Who was the last Nawab of Awadh, and on what grounds was Awadh annexed?
A
Nawab Wajid Ali Shah; annexed on grounds of misgovernance, 1856 ✔
✅ Answer: Wajid Ali Shah — last Nawab of Awadh. Awadh accepted the Subsidiary Alliance in 1801, stationing a permanent British force there. Dalhousie later prepared a report alleging poor governance, and Awadh was annexed in 1856 citing misgovernance

Famous Story — Wajid Ali Shah & Begum Hazrat Mahal:

  • Dalhousie called Awadh "a cherry ready to drop into the mouth of the British"
  • Wajid Ali Shah was taken away and imprisoned in Kolkata
  • His wife, Begum Hazrat Mahal, stayed back in Lucknow and later led the 1857 rebellion from Lucknow
✦ ✦ ✦
④ Immediate Cause — The Enfield Rifle Cartridge Controversy 🔫
Q4. What was the immediate/spark cause of the 1857 Revolt?
A
Introduction of the Enfield Rifle — greased cartridges rumored to contain cow & pig fat ✔
✅ Answer: The new Enfield Rifle used cartridges that had to be bitten open with the mouth. Rumors spread that the cartridge grease contained cow fat (sacred to Hindus) and pig fat (forbidden in Islam) — outraging soldiers of both religions

Racial & Structural Tensions (Underlying Causes):

  • Racial discrimination between British officers and Indian sepoys within the same army
  • Combination of religious, economic (land annexation), and military grievances created a "simmering" resentment — the cartridge issue was the spark that ignited it
✦ ✦ ✦
⑤ Outbreak — Mangal Pandey & the Spread of Revolt 🔥
Q5. Mangal Pandey belonged to which regiment, and what did he do?
A
34th Bengal Native Infantry — killed his officer in anger over the cartridge issue ✔
✅ Answer: Mangal Pandey, 34th Native Infantry (Bengal Native Infantry), killed his officer on March 29, 1857, enraged by the cartridge controversy. He was hanged on April 8, 1857
1857 Revolt — Full Outbreak Timeline
March 29, 1857 Mangal Pandey (34th Native Infantry) kills his officer over the cartridge issue
April 8, 1857 Mangal Pandey is hanged; news spreads rapidly among other regiments
April 24, 1857 Soldiers of the 3rd Native Cavalry (Meerut) refuse to use the new cartridges
May 9, 1857 ~90 soldiers refuse the cartridges; 85 are dismissed and sentenced to 10 years imprisonment
May 10, 1857 Enraged soldiers free their imprisoned comrades and begin marching toward Delhi — this date marks the formal start of the widespread revolt
May 1857 Rebels reach the Red Fort, Delhi and declare Bahadur Shah II (Bahadur Shah Zafar) as their ruler, rejecting British authority

Historical Irony: The Treaty of Allahabad (after the Battle of Buxar, 1765) had already brought the Mughals under British influence/pension. Yet in 1857, rebels still rallied around the aging Bahadur Shah II as a unifying symbolic ruler — showing his continued legitimacy in Indian eyes despite being powerless.

⑥ Delhi — Bahadur Shah II & General Bakht Khan 👑
Q6. Who was the symbolic leader of the revolt at Delhi, and who was the actual military commander leading operations there?
A
Symbolic: Bahadur Shah II (Zafar); Actual command: General Bakht Khan ✔
✅ Answer: Bahadur Shah II was declared the ruler/symbolic leader (he was old and not administratively capable). The actual military leadership was with General Bakht Khan, who was 81 years old and commanded operations from Delhi

Nana Saheb — Background Story:

  • Nana Saheb was the adopted son of Peshwa Baji Rao II
  • Baji Rao II's father figure connection traces back to Raghunath Rao, who signed the Treaty of Surat with the British, offering Salsette & Bassein in exchange for British help becoming Peshwa
  • Raghunath Rao was defeated by Nana Fadnavis; subsequent treaties followed: Treaty of Purandar (1776) → Treaty of Vadgaon → Treaty of Salbai
  • Like other rebel leaders, Nana Saheb's personal grievance stemmed from the British denying his adoptive succession rights under the Doctrine of Lapse

Rani Lakshmibai — Background Story:

  • Original name: Manikarnika Tambe ("Manu"); her father served under Peshwa Baji Rao II
  • Led the resistance from Jhansi, denied succession rights for her adopted son
  • Her memorial/samadhi is located at Phool Bagh, Gwalior

⑨ End of Mughal Rule & the Government of India Act, 1858 📜
Q8. What happened to Bahadur Shah II after the revolt was crushed?
A
Exiled to Rangoon Prison, Burma (Myanmar); the Mughal Empire was formally abolished ✔
✅ Answer: Bahadur Shah II was exiled to Rangoon (Rangoon Prison), Burma (present-day Myanmar) — living on a pension. This marked the formal end of the Mughal Empire, similar to how the Peshwa's rule had ended after the Third Anglo-Maratha War

East India Company — Timeline Recap (Before Its Abolition):

  • 1599 — Company formed in England
  • 1600 — Charter granted by Queen Elizabeth I for exclusive trade rights with India
  • 1608 — Hawkins arrives in India (first trade attempt)
  • 1615 — Thomas Roe arrives (secures trading rights from Jahangir)
  • 1757 — Battle of Plassey (start of political dominance)
  • 1858 — Company rule formally ends — nearly 100 years of political dominance from Plassey, over 250 years since its founding
Q9. Which Act abolished East India Company rule and transferred power directly to the British Crown?
A
Government of India Act, 1858 (also called the "Queen's Proclamation") ✔
✅ Answer: Government of India Act, 1858 — ended East India Company rule; established direct rule by the British Crown (Queen Victoria, with Lord Canning becoming the first Viceroy of India)

Government of India Act, 1858 — Key Provisions:

  • Abolished the East India Company
  • Converted the post of Governor-General of India into Viceroy — Canning became the first Viceroy
  • Created the post of Secretary of State for India, assisted by a 15-member Council
  • Abolished the Board of Control and Court of Directors (previously established by the Regulating Act of 1773 and modified via the Charter Act of 1833)
  • Established direct Crown rule — this is why 1858 marks the end of Company rule and start of the "British Raj" proper

⑩ Aftermath — Peel Commission & Army Reforms 🎖️
Q10. Which commission recommended increasing the ratio of British soldiers to Indian soldiers in the army, to prevent another uprising?
A
Peel Commission ✔
✅ Answer: Peel Commission — recommended fewer Indian soldiers and more British soldiers in the army, reasoning that a predominantly Indian army had enabled the 1857 revolt

⑪ Why the 1857 Revolt Failed — Analysis 🔍
Q11. Who termed the 1857 Revolt the "First War of Independence," and why do many historians dispute this label?
A
V.D. Savarkar; historians dispute it due to lack of unified national purpose ✔
✅ Answer: V.D. Savarkar called it the "First War of Independence." Many historians disagree because each leader fought for a regional/personal cause, not a unified national vision of independence

Reasons for Failure — Lack of Unity:

  • Rani Lakshmibai fought for Jhansi specifically
  • Begum Hazrat Mahal fought for the Awadh/Lucknow region
  • Nana Saheb fought to reclaim Maratha/Peshwa interests
  • No coordinated national leadership, no common political ideology, no shared long-term vision of "independence" as a unified concept

Reasons for Failure — Limited Support & Geographic Spread:

  • Zamindars (landlords) and the upper class largely did NOT support the revolt — many believed continued British rule would bring "civilization," education, and modernization
  • The revolt was concentrated mainly in North India (Delhi, Lucknow, Kanpur, Jhansi, Bareilly, Bihar) — South India saw virtually no involvement
  • No true "all-India" character despite the popular label

Balanced Perspective: While the "British brought civilization/education" argument has some historical basis for context (Europe was in its scientific/industrial rise — Newton, later Einstein — while India was fragmented by regional/Mughal conflicts), it does NOT excuse colonial exploitation. Modern research (e.g., Oxfam-style wealth-drain reports) has documented the scale of British extraction from India.

💡 Topper Tips — 1857 Revolt Ke Liye Yaad Rakho!
  • Doctrine of Lapse ≠ Awadh's annexation grounds — Awadh was annexed citing "misgovernance," NOT Doctrine of Lapse directly (a common trap!)
  • Mangal Pandey killed his officer on March 29, was hanged on April 8 — don't swap these dates
  • Symbolic leader at Delhi = Bahadur Shah II; actual military commander = General Bakht Khan — two different roles!
  • Nana Saheb AND Rani Lakshmibai both had personal grievances tied to the Doctrine of Lapse (denied adoption-based succession)
  • Leader-Suppressor pairs by region: Delhi (Nicholson), Lucknow (Lawrence), Kanpur (Campbell), Jhansi (Hugh Rose) — memorize as pairs!
  • The revolt was NOT all-India — concentrated in North India; South India was largely uninvolved
  • East India Company existed since 1599, but political dominance began only after Battle of Plassey, 1757 — nearly 100 years of dominance ended by 1858
  • V.D. Savarkar's "First War of Independence" label is disputed by historians due to lack of unified national purpose
