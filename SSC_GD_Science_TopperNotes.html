① Biology — Cell Biology & Cell Organelles 🧫
Q1. Cork cells contain which substance in their cell walls that makes them impermeable to gases and water?
A
Lignin
B
Chitin
C
Suberin ✔
D
Cutin
✅ Answer: C — Suberin makes cork cells (dead cells) impermeable to gases & water. Cork cells were first discovered by Robert Hooke in 1665

Cell Wall Substances — Don't Confuse!

  • Suberin — found in cork cells (dead cells); makes them impermeable
  • Cutin — secreted by epidermis of desert plants; builds up layer-by-layer to form the cuticle
  • Lignin — found in cell walls of xylem (sclerenchyma); slows down decomposition rate when present in detritus (broken down by detritivores)
  • Chitin — found in fungal cell walls AND in the exoskeleton of arthropods (largest animal phylum)
Q2. Which components of a plant's vascular system are responsible for water conduction?
A
Sieve tubes and companion cells
B
Vessels and Tracheae ✔
C
Guard cells and stomata
D
Phloem fibers
✅ Answer: B — Vessels and Tracheae (parts of xylem) conduct water & minerals. Phloem conducts food. Guard cells/stomata handle gas exchange & transpiration (water loss)
TissueFunctionComponents
XylemTransports water & mineralsVessels, Tracheae, Xylem parenchyma (only living part), Xylem fibers
PhloemTransports foodSieve tubes, Companion cells, Phloem fibers, Phloem parenchyma
🧠 Fiber = Strength (in both xylem & phloem). Parenchyma = Food Storage. All xylem tissues are DEAD except xylem parenchyma (living). All phloem tissues are LIVING except phloem fiber (dead)!

Plant Tissue Types — Overview:

  • Complex permanent tissue = Xylem & Phloem
  • Simple permanent tissue = Parenchyma (food storage; Aerenchyma = air spaces for floating), Collenchyma (flexibility; thickened at corners), Sclerenchyma (rigidity & toughness)
  • Meristematic tissue = site of cell division; Apical (root/shoot tips — increases height), Intercalary (between nodes — increases height), Lateral (increases girth/width)

Meristematic cells: lack vacuoles, have a prominent nucleus, and dense cytoplasm. When division stops, they differentiate into permanent tissue.

Tropisms — Positive vs Negative:

  • Phototropism — response to light; shoots show positive phototropism (grow toward light); roots show negative phototropism
  • Hydrotropism — response to water; roots show positive hydrotropism (grow toward water, downward); shoots show negative hydrotropism
  • Geotropism — response to gravity; same pattern as hydrotropism
  • Chemotropism — response to chemicals; e.g., pollen tube growing toward the ovule
Q7. Which of the following is an example of chemotropism in plants?
A
Growth of the pollen tube toward the ovule ✔
✅ Answer: The pollen tube grows toward the ovule (chemical signal-based movement) — this is chemotropism. Movements are of 2 types: Tropic (growth-related: photo/hydro/geo/chemo-tropism) & Nastic/Athetic (non-growth-related)
Pollination & Fertilization — Quick Flow
Step 1 Pollen grain (male part, from anther) lands on the sticky stigma (pollination)
Step 2 A pollen tube forms, growing toward the ovule (chemotropism)
Step 3 Pollen grain (male) meets ovule (female) → fertilization
Step 4 Ovule → becomes the seed; Ovary → becomes the fruit

⑧ Biology — DNA, Excretion & Misc 🧬

DNA — Structure & Replication:

  • Made of Genes (functional segments) + Histone proteins (hold DNA together)
  • Double helix structure; base pairs: A-T, G-C (in RNA, Uracil replaces Thymine)
  • Semi-conservative replication: Helicase unwinds → Primase starts → Ligase joins strands
  • Chromosome = DNA + Protein; Chromatid = duplicated chromosome strand
  • Humans: 46 chromosomes = 22 pairs autosomes + 1 pair sex chromosomes (XX = female, XY = male)
Q21. What are the principal nitrogenous waste products excreted by human kidneys?

Ozone Layer — Protocol Timeline

  • HCFCs (Hydrochlorofluorocarbons) — also ODS
  • Halons — release bromine (contain Br)

Sound — Key Facts:

  • Sensation of sound persists in the brain for about 0.1 second

㉑ Physics — Resistance, Density & Friction 🔧
Q67. A wire of resistance 4 ohms is stretched to double its length. What will its new resistance be?
A
4 ohms
B
8 ohms
C
12 ohms
D
16 ohms ✔
✅ Answer: D — 16 ohms. R = ρL/A. Doubling length (L→2L) AND halving area (A→A/2, since volume is conserved) → R becomes 4× the original = 4×4 = 16Ω
Resistance Formula: R = ρL/A (Resistance ∝ Length, Resistance ∝ 1/Area)
Stretching wire to double length → New R = 4 × Original R
Cutting wire in half (length halved, area unchanged) → New R = Original R ÷ 2
Q68. Water has maximum density at what temperature?
A
4°C ✔
✅ Answer: 4°C — this is the "Anomalous Expansion of Water." From 0°C to 4°C, water CONTRACTS (density increases); above 4°C, it expands normally (density decreases)
Q69. Which of the following statements about friction is correct?
A

B
Rolling friction is the smallest of all ✔
C
Friction depends on how hard two surfaces press together ✔
✅ All TRUE — correct order: Static > Sliding > Rolling friction (Rolling is the LEAST). Any option claiming otherwise (e.g., "friction can be increased by making surfaces smooth") would be the FALSE/incorrect statement to pick

Friction — Key Facts:

  • Order: Static > Sliding > Rolling friction (Rolling friction is LOWEST)
  • Friction depends on how hard two surfaces press together
  • Friction is essential for walking, cycling, driving (imagine walking on ice — no friction!)
  • Friction can be reduced by making surfaces smooth (e.g., using grease/lubricant); increased by making surfaces rough
