Land Revenue Systems — The Root of Discontent

In the name of "reform," the British began taxing land itself — land revenue: a share of whatever a farmer grew had to be paid to the Company. The British devised three separate systems to collect this revenue: the Permanent Settlement, the Ryotwari system, and the Mahalwari system.

1️⃣ Permanent Settlement (Zamindari System)

Introduced by Cornwallis in 1793, in the Bengal, Bihar & Orissa region. Zamindars (landowners) were appointed as intermediaries between the farmer and the British — the British avoided approaching farmers directly, hoping to keep "goodwill," while the zamindar did the actual, often harsh, collecting.

  • In return, zamindars received hereditary rights over the land — the right to collect revenue passed from father to son, generation after generation.
  • If a zamindar failed to deposit the fixed revenue by the set time, his landholding rights were taken away — this is the famous Sunset Clause ("Sunset Law").
  • That is why this system is also called the Zamindari System / the "landholding system."
  • It is called PERMANENT because the revenue amount, once fixed, was never revised again — contrast this with Ryotwari, where revenue was revised every 30 years.
📌 Did You Know?This hereditary right to collect revenue (passed father to son) is the same pattern behind hereditary landowning castes still found today — like the Deshmukhs & Fadnavis of Maharashtra!

2️⃣ Ryotwari System

Introduced by Munro and Read in 1820, in South India — mainly the Madras region (today's Tamil Nadu) and parts of Maharashtra. Based on the Ricardian Theory of Rent.

  • "Ryot" simply means peasant / farmer.
  • Unlike the Zamindari system, there was no middleman here — the British collected tax directly from the Ryot.
  • Revenue here was revised every 30 years, not fixed permanently.

3️⃣ Mahalwari System

Introduced in 1822 by Holt Mackenzie (who took it to the Bengal region) and William Bentinck (who extended it to Punjab).

  • "Mahal" means a group of villages.
  • Revenue was collected from the village headman — not the zamindar, not the individual farmer.
  • Crucially, the headman was chosen by the villagers themselves, not appointed by the British.
❌ Common Mistake Don't confuse who actually collects the revenue! Permanent Settlement → Zamindar collects (indirect). Ryotwari → British collect directly from the Ryot. Mahalwari → the village headman collects (chosen by villagers, not the British).

Why Did This Create So Much Anger?

Most of the areas under the Zamindari system were exactly the Bengal-Bihar-Orissa belt (today's Bengal, Bihar, Jharkhand & Bangladesh) — which is why the largest number of these early uprisings broke out right here.

  • Cornwallis also introduced the police inspector (daroga) system, so a police daroga would accompany the zamindar to enforce collection.
  • If the harvest was poor, farmers had to borrow from moneylenders — often the zamindars themselves, wearing a second hat.
  • Interest rates were so high that farmers could never repay even the interest, let alone the principal.
  • Inability to repay pushed many into bonded labour.
  • This deep resentment against zamindars and the British — among both farmers and tribal communities — fed directly into the wave of revolts covered on the next five pages.
👮 Why Villagers Still Fear the Daroga (tap to expand)
Cornwallis introduced the police inspector (daroga) system to help zamindars enforce collection. Even today, in many villages, people fear the local police daroga more than a senior IPS officer — because it is the daroga who actually shows up in person to collect and enforce, week after week.
Farmer→ Zamindar + Daroga collect→ Can't Pay→ Moneylender (high interest)→ Bonded Labour
💡 Understand it like this...A farmer with a bad harvest tells the zamindar, "I don't have the money." So he borrows from a moneylender instead — often the same zamindar wearing a different hat! The interest is so high he can never repay even the interest, let alone the principal → bonded labour.
🧠 Memory TrickThe system's own name tells you who pays! Ryotwari → the Ryot (peasant) pays directly. Mahalwari → the Mahal (village group) pays via its headman. Zamindari → the Zamindar is the middleman who actually hands over the money.

Sanyasi-Fakir Rebellion & the Paika Rebellion

🙏 The Sanyasi-Fakir Rebellion

After the Battle of Buxar (1764), where the British defeated Shah Alam II, Shuja-ud-Daula & Mir Qasim, the Company took firm control of Bengal — and with it, the Mughal and Awadh courts.

  • Wandering Sanyasis (Hindu ascetics) and Fakirs (Muslim ascetics) had long travelled on pilgrimage with their expenses borne by the local kingdom/court — a form of royal patronage.
  • The British refused to continue this patronage, and instead declared them "bandits."
  • Angered, they revolted, growing through the 1770s.
  • Leaders: Manju Shah, Bhawani Pathak, and Devi Chaudhurani (a notable female participant/leader).
  • Region: Bengal.

Both events are dramatised in novels by Bankim Chandra Chatterjee: Anandmath and Devi Chaudhurani.

⚔️ The Paika Rebellion

Year: 1817. Region: Khurda, Odisha. Leader: Bakshi Jagabandhu Vidyadhar, military commander of the Khurda Kingdom.

  • The Odisha (Gajapati) rulers of Khurda had come under British control by accepting the Subsidiary Alliance.
  • Subsidiary Alliance: introduced by Wellesley in 1798; its terms required the kingdom to disband its own local army and accept British troops instead — at the kingdom's expense.
  • The Nizam of Hyderabad was the first ruler to accept the Subsidiary Alliance (1798).
  • When Khurda's local paik soldiers were disbanded and replaced, Bakshi Jagabandhu led them in revolt (1817).
  • In recent years, the Odisha government has sought to have the Paika Rebellion officially recognised as the "First War of Independence," but the Central Government has not accepted this, arguing the revolt was fought to restore the Khurda kingdom rather than for a united national independence.
⚔️ The Carnatic Wars — Dupleix, Coote & Wandiwash (tap to expand)
The Subsidiary Alliance was originally Dupleix's brainchild — the French Governor-General who led French forces in the First & Second Carnatic Wars. He was recalled after the British complained he was "creating trouble." In the Third Carnatic War, the British were led by Eyre Coote and the French by Comte de Lally. At the Battle of Wandiwash (1760), Coote defeated Lally.

The Ahom Revolt & the Pagalpanthi

🐘 The Ahom Revolt (Assam)

Led by Gomdhar Konwar in 1828. This followed the First Anglo-Burmese War (1824–1826), which ended with the Treaty of Yandaboo (1826).

  • Before the treaty, the Ahom Kingdom (Assam, and part of today's Bangladesh) was under the influence of the Burmese (Myanmar) empire.
  • Under the Treaty of Yandaboo, this region came under British control, and the British promised they would withdraw.
  • They did not leave — two years passed with no withdrawal.
  • Gomdhar Konwar led a revolt against this broken promise in 1828.
  • The Company suppressed the revolt, but — having made an impression — chose not to hold the territory outright: they returned the territory to the Ahoms and withdrew.

🕉️ The Pagalpanthi (Bengal)

A movement started around 1825 by Karam Shah and his son Tipu Shah, in the Bengal region.

  • Tipu Shah established a distinct sect — a bit like how Akbar had once established Din-i-Ilahi: a new set of beliefs not fitting neatly into existing Hindu or Muslim orthodoxy.
  • Karam Shah appealed to both Hindus and Muslims, urging orthodox believers to drop certain rigid practices and pointing out flaws on both sides.
  • People mockingly called them "mad" ("Pagal") — hence Pagalpanthi.
  • It is studied here because the movement gradually turned into a revolt against local landlords.
🗡️ Battle of Saraighat, 1671 (tap to expand)
Long before the British arrived, the Ahom Kingdom had already defended itself against the Mughals in a conflict stretching back to the 1620s, climaxing at the Battle of Saraighat (1671). Mughal forces were led by Ram Singh (Emperor Aurangzeb did not personally take part); Ahom forces were led by Lachit Borphukan. The Mughals were defeated. In his honour, the National Defence Academy's top cadet trophy is named the Lachit Borphukan Award.

Kol Mutiny & the Munda Rebellion (Ulgulan)

⛏️ The Kol Mutiny

Led by Buddhu Bhagat in 1831. A tribal uprising in the Chota Nagpur Plateau region, involving the Ho and Munda tribes.

🪓 The Munda Rebellion — "Ulgulan"

The British wanted revenue, and this forest-rich plateau (though its mineral wealth wasn't the British focus at the time — agriculture was) was a target. They wanted to clear tribal forest land for cultivation, so agricultural tax could be earned.

  • Led by Birsa Munda, who led the Ho and Munda tribes' uprising.
  • Born 15 November 1875; began leading the movement around the age of 20, i.e. c. 1895.
  • His birthday, 15 November, is now observed as Tribal Pride Day.
  • Directed against the "Diku" — outsiders, specifically outsider landlords who arrived once agriculture (and zamindari-style revenue collection) came to tribal land.
  • The Munda community upheld the Khuntkatti system — collective/joint land ownership, with no single owner: the community would grow, eat and live together with a strong communal bond. The movement also carried a religious dimension, resisting outside religious influence.
  • Also called the Ulgulan ("uprising") — commonly explained as a call to overthrow oppressive rule. Many tribal groups still invoke the word today.
  • The uprising began around 1895; Birsa Munda was captured in 1899 and died in jail in 1900 — this account attributes his death to slow poisoning by the British.
🗣️ "Ulgulan!" — the Munda rallying cry, meaning roughly "rise up, overthrow the oppressor" — still invoked by tribal groups today.

🏆 Birsa Munda's Legacy Today (tap to expand)
Birsa Munda is worshipped as a deity ("Bhagwan Birsa Munda") by tribal communities today. Ranchi's airport is named Birsa Munda Airport after him, and one of India's largest hockey stadiums also carries his name. His birthday (15 November) is both Tribal Pride Day and the date Jharkhand was formed, in 2000.

The Santhal Rebellion & the Moplah Rebellion

🏹 The Santhal Rebellion

Studied here among the Tribal Uprisings, though it falls chronologically right before 1857 itself — began in 1855, led by Sidhu and Kanhu Murmu (two of four Murmu brothers; the other two aren't essential for exams).

  • The Santhal tribe is India's third-largest tribal group, after the Bhil and Gond tribes.
  • India's current President, Droupadi Murmu — the country's first tribal President — belongs to this tribe.
  • Around 1832, the British demarcated a forest tract of the Chota Nagpur Plateau called Damin-i-Koh for cultivation, and brought in the Santhal tribe (believed to trace its origins to the Cambodia/Southeast Asia region) to clear this forest and farm it.
  • Once settled, a zamindar was placed over the Santhals, collecting tax and rent — and the same trap of moneylenders and high interest followed, just as on Page 1.
  • The Santhals fought hard, but had only bows and arrows against British artillery — they could not win militarily.
  • The British ultimately overpowered them; local kings and officials also sided with the British.
⚔️ Why the Rebellion Ultimately Failed (tap to expand)
The Santhals fought with courage and organisation, but they had no artillery to match the British — only bows and arrows. On top of this military gap, several local kings and officials chose to side with the British rather than the Santhals, which tipped the balance decisively against the rebellion.

🕌 The Moplah Rebellion

Year: 1836 (a separate, much larger Moplah uprising also occurred in 1921, studied separately). Region: Malabar.

  • The Moplah (Mappila) are a Muslim community of the Malabar region.
  • In 1836, they revolted against Hindu landlords.

The Indigo Revolt & the Road to 1857

Indigo ("neel") is a plant that yields a natural blue dye — no longer common today since synthetic dyes replaced it. The plant demands far more water than rice, and gradually makes the soil unfertile. The British wanted it anyway, to sell at a good rate in the European market.

🪤 The "Advance Payment" Trap

  • Farmers preferred growing wheat and rice for their own food — indigo brought no food and ruined the soil.
  • So the British devised a system: sign an agreement, and pay the farmer in advance, before the crop was even planted.
  • Drawn in by the immediate money, cultivators signed without reading or understanding the contract's terms — this was a trap: it bound them to keep growing indigo.
  • With no way out, the planters revolted in 1859.
📜 How the "Advance Payment" Trap Worked (tap to expand)
The British signed contracts with farmers and paid them in advance — before the crop was even in the ground. Farmers, drawn by the immediate cash, signed without understanding the contract's actual terms. Once signed, those terms bound them to keep growing indigo season after season, trapping them in a cycle they could not escape.

✊ The Revolt & Its Voice

Leaders: Digambar Biswas and Bishnu Biswas. The revolt was dramatised in the play Neel Darpan ("Blue/Indigo Mirror") by Dinabandhu Mitra — its impact was so great that the British banned the play.
