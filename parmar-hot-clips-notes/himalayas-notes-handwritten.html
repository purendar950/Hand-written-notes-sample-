How the Himalayas Were Formed

About 150 million years ago, the supercontinent Pangaea (surrounded by the ocean Panthalassa) broke into two: Laurasia on top and Gondwanaland on the bottom. India was part of Gondwanaland. India then drifted upward toward the Eurasian Plate.

🧠 Fig 1: Formation of the Himalayas

Both the Indian Plate and the Eurasian Plate are continental plates of similar density — so neither was willing to sink under the other. This is called continent–continent convergence, and it produced fold mountains: the Himalayas.

The Tethys Sea — The Evidence

Between the two colliding plates lay a sea called the Tethys Sea. We know it existed because marine fossils have been found high up in the mountains — sea sediments don't get there unless a sea once existed in that spot. The Himalayas were not formed by a sudden collapse; they built up slowly, from sea-bed to peak, over millions of years.

🎯 Exam Tip The presence of marine fossils in the high Himalayas is the classic evidence question — always link it to the vanished Tethys Sea.
❌ Common Mistake Don't picture the Himalayas as forming in one dramatic "collapse" — the process was gradual, and it's still ongoing: the range's height increases by roughly 5 cm every year.
🧠 Memory TrickPangaea → splits → Laurasia (north) + Gondwanaland (south, with India) → India drifts up → hits Eurasia → Tethys Sea squeezed out → Himalayas rise.

The Four Divisions & the Trans-Himalaya

From north to south, the Himalayas are divided into four ranges: the Trans-Himalaya, the Great Himalaya, the Lesser Himalaya, and the Shivalik.

Karakoram Glaciers

In the Karakoram range, remember these glaciers: Hispar, Baltoro, Biafo, and Siachen.

🧠 Memory Trick Just remember "H-B-B-S" for the Karakoram glaciers: Hispar, Baltoro, Biafo, Siachen.

In 1984, Operation Meghdoot took place at the Siachen Glacier. Siachen is the second-longest non-polar glacier in the world — the longest is the Fedchenko Glacier in Tajikistan.

❌ Common Mistake Don't say Siachen is the longest non-polar glacier — it's the second-longest; the Fedchenko Glacier in Tajikistan holds the top spot.
🎯 Exam Tip A "mountain range" simply means a whole connected series of mountains — keep this basic definition ready for direct-definition questions.

The Great Himalaya (Himadri)

The Great Himalaya is the oldest of the four ranges, also called the Inner Himalaya or Himadri. This is where the world's highest peaks are found. Its core is made of granitic rock, and the average height of its peaks is 6000 m. Its western-most point is Nanga Parbat; the range's eastern-most extent reaches into Arunachal Pradesh.

📖 Wait — isn't K2 India's highest peak? (tap to expand)
K2 (height 8611 m, world's 2nd highest peak, also called Godwin Austen) is NOT part of the Great Himalaya — it belongs to the Karakoram range, and lies in the PoK (Pakistan-occupied Kashmir) area, making its status disputed. Kanchenjunga, which lies in Sikkim — proper Indian territory — is therefore recognised as India's highest peak.
❌ Common Mistake Don't club K2 with the Great Himalaya's top peaks — it's a Karakoram (Trans-Himalaya) peak, in a disputed PoK location, not part of the Great Himalaya range at all.
🎯 Exam Tip Order to remember: Everest (world #1) → K2 (world #2, Karakoram) → Kanchenjunga (world #3, India's highest) → Lhotse (world #4).

Lesser Himalaya, Shivalik & the Valleys

Lesser Himalaya (Himachal) — Regional Names

The Lesser (Middle) Himalaya has an average height of 4000 m and is known by different local names across regions:

RegionLocal Name of the Range
KashmirPir Panjal Range
Himachal PradeshDhauladhar Range
UttarakhandNag Tibba Range
NepalMahabharat Range
❌ Common Mistake Don't confuse Dhauladhar (the Lesser Himalaya range in Himachal Pradesh) with Dhaulagiri (a Great Himalaya peak in Nepal) — similar-sounding names, completely different things!

Shivalik (Outer Himalaya)

The Shivalik range is the youngest of the four divisions. It disappears in the Northeast, where it's replaced by the Duars — soft, unconsolidated deposits.

🗺️ Fig 4: Where the Valleys Sit

Kashmir Valley & Karewa

The Kashmir Valley lies between the Great Himalaya and the Lesser Himalaya. Here you find Karewa — soft, unconsolidated glacial/lacustrine deposits — which are especially helpful for growing saffron (a local variety).

Duns

Duns are longitudinal valleys found between the Lesser Himalaya and the Shivalik. The largest Dun is Dehradun; another named Dun is Patli Dun.

Uttarakhand's Grasslands & Tribes

Summer grasslands in Uttarakhand are called Bugyal — home to the Bhotia tribe. The Gujjar and Bakarwal tribes are also found here.

🎯 Exam Tip Keep the valley-location pairs clear: Karewa → Kashmir Valley (between Great & Lesser Himalaya); Duns → between Lesser Himalaya & Shivalik.
🧠 Memory TrickKarewa = Kashmir's saffron soil; Duns = Dehradun's valley soil — both are soft, unconsolidated deposits, just in different locations!

Regional (Longitudinal) Divisions & Purvanchal

The Himalayas are also divided longitudinally, based on the rivers that mark their boundaries:

Between RiversHimalaya Name
Indus – SutlejPunjab Himalaya
Sutlej – KaliKumaon Himalaya
Kali – TeestaGarhwal / Nepal Himalaya
Teesta – BrahmaputraAssam Himalaya
🧠 Memory Trick Think "IPS" — Indus → Punjab Himalaya → Sutlej — to lock in the first segment, then move river by river: Sutlej–Kali (Kumaon), Kali–Teesta (Garhwal/Nepal), Teesta–Brahmaputra (Assam).

Focus on which Himalaya lies between which two rivers — exact locations aren't usually asked, the river-pairing is what matters.

Purvanchal Himalaya (Northeast)

Beyond the Assam Himalaya, in the Northeast, lie the Purvanchal Himalayas — a set of hill ranges across Arunachal Pradesh and the northeastern states:

🗺️ Fig 5: The Purvanchal Hill Ranges
Dafla→ Miri→ Abor→ Mishmi→ Nishi→ Patkai Bum→ Barail Range→ Naga Hills→ Manipur Hills→ Mizo (Lushai) Hills
📜 "Mizo Lushai is the same" — Mizo Hills are also known as the Lushai Hills.
🎯 Exam Tip Write the Purvanchal chain out by hand a few times — Dafla, Miri, Abor, Mishmi, Nishi, Patkai Bum, Barail, Naga, Manipur, Mizo (Lushai) — repetition is the only trick that works here.
❌ Common Mistake Don't drop the alternate name — Mizo Hills = Lushai Hills; exams may use either name for the same range.

⭐ Exam-Ready Summary

  • Formation: Continent–continent convergence between the Indian & Eurasian plates; Tethys Sea evidence via marine fossils; still rising ~5 cm/year
  • Four divisions (oldest→youngest): Great Himalaya → Lesser Himalaya → Shivalik (Trans-Himalaya sits apart, from Tethys deposits)
  • Trans-Himalaya order: Karakoram → Ladakh → Zanskar; glaciers: Hispar, Baltoro, Biafo, Siachen
  • Great Himalaya: Himadri/Inner Himalaya; Everest, Kanchenjunga, Lhotse, Makalu, Dhaulagiri, Nanda Devi, Kamet (K2 excluded — it's Karakoram)
  • Lesser Himalaya names: Pir Panjal (Kashmir), Dhauladhar (HP), Nag Tibba (Uttarakhand), Mahabharat (Nepal)
  • Valleys: Kashmir Valley (Karewa, saffron) between Great & Lesser Himalaya; Duns (Dehradun) between Lesser Himalaya & Shivalik
  • Longitudinal divisions: Punjab, Kumaon, Garhwal/Nepal, Assam Himalaya — plus Purvanchal in the Northeast
