Where It All Began — Nagaur, 1959

On 2 October 1959, Panchayat provisions were first implemented in the Nagaur district of Rajasthan. But at this stage, Panchayats were not constitutional in nature — they were not mandatory anywhere.

To make Panchayats mandatory across the country, a constitutional amendment was eventually needed — the 73rd Amendment. But before that amendment, several committees were set up to study and recommend how Panchayati Raj should work.

🧠 Fig 1: Committees That Shaped Panchayati Raj

1️⃣ Balwant Rai Mehta Committee — 1957

This was the first committee to recommend that a three-tier Panchayati Raj system should be implemented across India.

2️⃣ Ashok Mehta Committee — 1977

Formed by Ashok Mehta in 1977. It disagreed with the three-tier idea and said a two-tier system is enough.

❌ Common Mistake Don't get the two mixed up! Balwant Rai Mehta (1957) = 3-tier system. Ashok Mehta (1977) = said 2-tier is enough. Opposite recommendations, 20 years apart!

3️⃣ L.M. Singhvi Committee

Recommended giving Panchayats constitutional status, along with a focus on their revitalization.

💡 What is "revitalization" here?It simply means strengthening and reviving the working of Panchayats — not literally reviving anything with a "pill"! It's about making the existing Panchayat system function better.

4️⃣ Thungon Committee

Also examined the working and revitalization of Panchayats, in line with the points above.

🧠 Memory Trick Balwant Rai → Begins with 3-tier. Ashok Mehta → After him, only 2-tier. Singhvi → Status (constitutional). Thungon → Together with revitalization.
🎯 Exam Tip Remember the sequence: Nagaur (1959, first implementation, not mandatory) → committees review the working → 73rd Amendment (1992) makes it constitutional and mandatory.

The 73rd Constitutional Amendment, 1992

After reviewing all these committees, an amendment was made — the 73rd Constitutional Amendment, passed in 1992. P.V. Narasimha Rao was the Prime Minister at that time.

But the Amendment only came into force on 24 April 1993. That is why Panchayati Raj Day is celebrated on 24 April every year.

🗓️ Fig 2: From Nagaur to the 73rd Amendment

What the 73rd Amendment Added

  • Part Nine (Part IX) was added to the Constitution
  • Articles 243 to 243O were added under Part IX
  • The 11th Schedule was added to the Constitution
  • Panchayats now officially received constitutional status
📖 Understand it like this...Before 1992, Panchayats existed only because individual states chose to set them up — like a rule that could be changed anytime. After the 73rd Amendment, Panchayats became a constitutional requirement — no state can simply choose to skip them.

Article 243 — The Gram Sabha

Article 243 first talks about the Gram Sabha — a body at the most basic/village level. It consists of all persons whose names are on the electoral (voter) list of that village. In other words, everyone who can vote in that village is automatically a member of the Gram Sabha.

🎯 Exam Tip Gram Sabha is not elected — it is simply the entire body of registered voters of the village meeting together. Don't confuse it with the elected Gram Panchayat!

243B–243J — Structure, Powers & Finance

Article 243B — Constitution of Panchayats

This article tells us how the Panchayat will be constituted — India follows a three-tier Panchayati Raj system.

🏗️ Fig 3: The Three-Tier Panchayati Raj System
  • Gram Panchayat — village level
  • Panchayat Samiti — block level (also called the intermediate level)
  • Zila Parishad — district level
❌ Common Mistake Where the population is less than 2 million (20 lakh), it is not necessary to create the intermediate (block) level — the state may skip it.

Article 243C — Composition of Panchayat

How are the seats filled? All seats of the Panchayat are filled through direct elections.

  • At the intermediate (block) and district level, the Chairman is elected indirectly
  • At the village level, the manner of choosing the Chairman is as determined by the State Legislature
🧠 Memory TrickAll Panchayat seats = direct election. Only the Chairman's seat changes rule: Intermediate + District Chairman = indirect; Village Chairman = "as the State Legislature wishes."

Article 243D — Reservation of Seats

  • Women: 1/3rd of the total seats are reserved
  • SC / ST: reserved in proportion to their population
🎯 Exam Tip Railways (NTPC) and SSC exams frequently ask about local bodies — Panchayat & Municipality reservation rules are a favourite question area. Study these numbers carefully!

Article 243E — Duration of Panchayats

The duration of a Panchayat is 5 years. If a Panchayat is prematurely dissolved, the newly-formed Panchayat serves only for the remainder of the original term — not a fresh full 5-year term.

Example: A Panchayat formed in 2020 has its term till 2025. If it gets dissolved early — say in 2022 — the new Panchayat that is formed will serve only until 2025 (the remainder of the term), not a fresh 5 years.

❌ Common Mistake This "remainder of term only" rule is unique to the Panchayat — it does not happen with the Lok Sabha or a Legislative Assembly, where a fresh dissolution leads to a fresh full term.

Article 243F — Disqualification of Membership

  • Minimum age required to contest is only 21 years — not 25 years
  • The authority which decides disqualification is determined by the State Legislature

Articles 243G, H, I, J

  • 243G: Powers, authority and responsibilities are given to the Panchayat by the State Legislature
  • 243H: Power to impose taxes & manage funds — again determined by the State Legislature
  • 243I: A Finance Commission is constituted — this is the State's own Finance Commission (do not confuse with Article 280's Union Finance Commission)
  • 243J: Deals with Audit of Panchayat accounts
🧠 Memory Trick — A to J Assembly (Sabha) → Banana (Constitution) → Composition → Dalit (Reservation) → E means five (Duration) → Fail (Disqualify) → G means bullet (Power/authority) → House Tax → Income (Finance) → J Investigation (Audit).

Elections (243K–243O) & the PESA Act

Article 243K — Elections

Elections to the Panchayat are conducted by the State Election Commission, which is formed specifically for this purpose.

Article 243L — Application to Union Territories

This article extends the provisions of Part IX to Union Territories (UTs).

Article 243M — Part Not to Apply to Certain Areas

The provisions of Part IX do not apply in certain areas — keep in mind Nagaland, Meghalaya and Mizoram.

Article 243O — Bar to Interference by Courts

This article means that courts cannot interfere in matters relating to Panchayat elections.

🎯 Exam Tip Quick recall for the last few articles: K=Elections(State Election Commission), L=UTs, M=Not applicable in Nagaland/Meghalaya/Mizoram, O=courts can't interfere in elections.

📜 The PESA Act, 1996

The Panchayat (Extension to Scheduled Areas) Act was passed in 1996. Under this Act, the provisions of Part IX cannot be directly applied in Scheduled Areas — they apply only with certain modifications.

🧠 Memory TrickPESA = Panchayat's Extension to Scheduled Areas → think "Part IX, but customised for tribal areas."
  • The Gram Panchayat was given a lot of power in Scheduled (Fifth Schedule) Areas
  • All Chairman-level seats are reserved for tribals
  • At other levels, 50% of seats are reserved for tribals
  • Purpose: to promote self-governance in tribal areas
🧠 Memory Trick PESA reservation split: Chairman = 100% tribal, other seats = 50% tribal — remember "Top post fully tribal, rest half tribal."
❌ Common Mistake PESA does not apply Part IX word-for-word to Scheduled Areas — it applies it with modifications, giving extra powers like near-total tribal reservation.

🔑 Key Facts at a Glance

  • First implementation: Nagaur, Rajasthan — 2 October 1959 (not mandatory then)
  • Constitutional Amendment: 73rd Amendment, 1992 (PM: P.V. Narasimha Rao)
  • Came into force: 24 April 1993 → celebrated as Panchayati Raj Day
  • Added to Constitution: Part IX, Articles 243–243O, 11th Schedule
  • Structure: Gram Panchayat (village) → Panchayat Samiti (block) → Zila Parishad (district)
  • Reservation: Women 1/3; SC/ST in proportion to population
  • Duration: 5 years; premature dissolution → only remainder of term for the new body
  • Minimum age to contest: 21 years
  • Elections conducted by: State Election Commission (243K)
  • Not applicable in: Nagaland, Meghalaya, Mizoram (243M)
  • PESA Act, 1996: applies Part IX to Scheduled Areas with modifications; Chairman seats 100% tribal, other seats 50% tribal
