What Is a Constitutional Amendment?

A constitutional amendment means changing, adding, deleting, or replacing something in our Constitution — the largest written constitution in the world. The State Legislature cannot do this; only the country's Parliament can. The bill can be introduced in either the Lok Sabha or the Rajya Sabha (never in a State Legislative Assembly or Council).

The entire procedure is laid out in Part XX (Part 20) of the Constitution, under Article 368. This procedure was borrowed from the Constitution of South Africa.

🧠 Fig 1: How a Constitutional Amendment Bill Moves

Three Unique Rules for a Constitution Amendment Bill

  • No joint sitting — unlike ordinary bills, a Constitution Amendment Bill has no provision for a joint sitting of both Houses if there's a deadlock (joint sitting itself comes from Article 108, borrowed from Australia)
  • No prior Presidential recommendation is required to introduce it — contrast this with a Money Bill, which does need the President's prior recommendation
  • President's assent is mandatory — he cannot return it, cannot withhold assent, and cannot "pocket" it. This is the only type of bill where the President has no veto power at all
❌ Common Mistake Don't mix up Money Bills and Constitution Amendment Bills — a Money Bill needs the President's prior recommendation before introduction; a Constitution Amendment Bill does not.
🎯 Exam Tip Remember these three together: No joint sitting + No prior recommendation + Mandatory Presidential assent — classic 1-mark questions on Constitution Amendment Bills.

K. C. Wheare's Description

The scholar K. C. Wheare, who wrote extensive commentary on our Constitution's provisions, described India's federal structure as "quasi-federal". In the same way, he described India's amending procedure as neither wholly rigid nor wholly flexible — i.e., "partly rigid, partly flexible."

🧠 Memory TrickQuasi-federal (structure) + Partly rigid, partly flexible (amending procedure) — both phrases come from the same scholar, K. C. Wheare!
🤔 Food for ThoughtThe lecture itself poses this as an open question: besides the Constitution Amendment Bill, is there another type of bill where joint sitting is also not allowed? Worth researching further!

Simple, Special & Special+Ratification Majority

Article 368 covers two types of amendment: by special majority, and by special majority plus ratification from the states. There's also a simple majority route for some provisions — but that is not treated as an amendment "under Article 368" at all; these are called the flexible parts of the Constitution.

Majority TypeDefinition (as taught)
Simple Majority2/3 of members present and voting in the House — not counted as an Article 368 amendment
Special MajorityMajority of the total membership of the House, AND 2/3 of members present and voting — whichever number is higher must be achieved
Special Majority + RatificationSpecial majority in Parliament + ratification by at least half the states (simple majority in each state legislature) — e.g. out of 28 states, at least 14 states

Needs only Simple Majority (not an Art 368 amendment): Second Schedule, Third Schedule, Parliament's own Rules of Procedure, provisions of Citizenship, creation or abolition of a Legislative Council, and any change under Articles 2 and 3 (creating a new state, altering boundaries) — because Article 4 says such changes are not considered an amendment under Article 368.

🎯 Exam Tip Group the "ratification" list together: Supreme/High Courts, Presidential election, Seventh Schedule, Article 368 itself, Representation of States — all federal-structure-sensitive, hence states get a say.
❌ Common Mistake Don't assume Fundamental Rights or DPSP amendments need state ratification — they only need a special majority, no state involvement required.
🧠 Memory Trick"Court-President-Schedule-368-Representation" — five things so central to India's federal structure that states must ratify any change to them.

📖 The Berubari Union Case (tap to expand)
Under the Nehru-Noon Agreement between Jawaharlal Nehru and Pakistan's PM Feroz Khan Noon, India decided to give the Berubari Union to Pakistan (in territory that is today Bangladesh). This was challenged in the Berubari Union case (1960), where the Supreme Court ruled that India could acquire new territory by a simple majority (per Article 4), but giving away Indian territory required a full constitutional amendment — hence the 9th Amendment.
❌ Common Mistake Acquiring territory (like Dadra & Nagar Haveli, Goa, Puducherry) only needs a simple majority under Article 4 — but giving away Indian territory (like Berubari) needs a full constitutional amendment. Don't treat the two as the same process!
🎯 Exam Tip Goa, Dadra & Nagar Haveli, and Daman & Diu were ruled by the Portuguese; Puducherry was ruled by the French — the Portuguese were driven out via Operation Vijay (1961).

🧠 Fig 4: Golaknath → 24th → 25th → Kesavananda Bharati
❌ Common Mistake Article 31C's DPSP-immunity clause and its judicial-review-ouster clause are two different things — the Supreme Court in Kesavananda Bharati upheld the first but struck down the second.
📖 What was the Privy Purse? (tap to expand)
Princely states had the freedom to join India, join Pakistan, or remain independent. Sardar Patel persuaded many to join India by promising them an annual payment for doing nothing further — this payment was called the Privy Purse. Indira Gandhi abolished this system in 1971 through the 26th Amendment.
🎯 Exam Tip Don't confuse the 26th Amendment (Privy Purse abolished) with the 19th Amendment of 1966 (Election Tribunals abolished) — a very common mix-up in competitive exams (see Page 5).
🧠 Memory Trick1st Amendment = land reforms protected via the 9th Schedule; 24th & 25th Amendments = Parliament pushing back on the judiciary over Fundamental Rights.

❌ Common Mistake A frequently-confused exam question: "What was removed by the 19th Amendment?" The correct answer is Election Tribunals — not the Privy Purse (that was the 26th Amendment).

The Emergency (1975) & the 42nd Amendment — "Mini-Constitution"

Indira Gandhi declared a National Emergency in 1975 on the ground of "internal disturbance", without consulting her own Cabinet, directing President Fakhruddin Ali Ahmed to impose it, and jailing many opposition politicians. The 42nd Amendment (1976) that followed made so many changes it is called a "mini-Constitution."

📜 The 42nd Amendment is remembered as a "mini-Constitution" for its sheer scale of changes.
🧠 Fig 5: The 42nd Amendment — A Mini-Constitution

All the Changes the 42nd Amendment Made

  • Added three words to the Preamble: Socialist, Secular, Integrity
  • Added new DPSPs: Article 39A (free legal aid to the poor), Article 43A (workers' participation in management of industries), Article 48A (protection of environment)
  • Added Fundamental Duties for the first time, in Part IV-A, Article 51A
  • Made the President bound by Cabinet advice under Article 74 — he could not ask for reconsideration
  • Gave judicial powers to Administrative Tribunals; added Part XIV-A for tribunal matters (central government employees' disputes, and other matters)
  • Tried to extend Article 31C's immunity to cover all DPSPs (not just 39(b)/(c)) — this extension was later struck down by the Supreme Court
  • Shifted subjects from the State List to the Concurrent List: Education, Forests, Weights and Measures, Administration of Justice, Protection of Wild Animals
  • Union List subjects increased from 97 to 100; State List decreased from 66 to 61; Concurrent List subjects increased from 47 (final count not detailed in the lecture)

The 44th Amendment — Correcting the 42nd

  • Made the Cabinet's advice to the President returnable once for reconsideration — binding only after reconsideration
  • Restored the Fundamental Rights that had been curtailed during the Emergency
  • Removed Right to Property from Part III (Fundamental Rights), moving it to Article 300A in Part XII as a mere legal right — enforceable only via a High Court writ, not the Supreme Court
  • Added the word "Cabinet" into the Constitution's text for the first time
  • Declaring a National Emergency now needs a special majority in Parliament, not a simple majority
  • Replaced the ground "internal disturbance" with "armed rebellion" under Article 352
🎯 Exam Tip This is exactly why the High Court's writ jurisdiction is wider than the Supreme Court's — legal rights (like the post-44th Right to Property) can only be enforced by a High Court writ.
🧠 Memory Trick42nd Amendment = Parliament/Executive gains more power (Emergency era); 44th Amendment = course-correction, restoring checks and balances.

52nd to 106th — The Anti-Defection Law Onwards

52nd Amendment (1985) — Anti-Defection Law

Added the Tenth Schedule, dealing with disqualification on grounds of defection. It applies when:

  • An independent member (contested without a party) joins a political party after being elected
  • A nominated member (e.g., in the Rajya Sabha) joins a party after six months
  • A member leaves their party and joins another

Exception: if 2/3 or more of a party's members leave together, it's treated as a "merger," not a "split" — and a merger is not covered by the anti-defection law.

🧠 Fig 6: When Does the Anti-Defection Law Apply?
❌ Common Mistake Don't assume every mass party exit counts as "defection" — if 2/3 or more members leave together (like the Eknath Shinde example in Maharashtra), it's legally a merger, and the anti-defection law does not apply.

📖 Not a Constitutional Amendment — the Waqf Law Change (tap to expand)
Parliament also replaced the Waqf Act, 1995 with a new law popularly called the UMEED Act, 2025 (Unified Waqf Management, Empowerment, and Development). This is an ordinary Act of Parliament, not a Constitutional Amendment — so it should not be confused with the 107th Constitutional Amendment or any similar numbering.

⭐ Exam-Ready Summary

  • Amendment procedure: Part XX, Article 368 — borrowed from South Africa
  • Who can amend: only Parliament, never a State Legislature
  • Two types under Art 368: Special majority; Special majority + state ratification (≥ half the states)
  • Outside Art 368: simple-majority "flexible" provisions (e.g., citizenship rules, new states)
  • Unique CAB rules: no joint sitting, no prior Presidential recommendation, mandatory Presidential assent
  • Most amended era: 42nd (1976) "mini-Constitution" during the Emergency, corrected by the 44th (1978)
  • Most recent: 106th Amendment (2023) — 33% women's reservation, pending implementation
🎯 Exam Tip If an MCQ names an amendment number and a change, check the era first: 1st–14th = early Republic/territory; 15th–26th = courts/rights tug-of-war; 35th–44th = Sikkim/Emergency; 52nd onward = defection law to the present day.
