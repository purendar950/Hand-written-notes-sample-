Setting the Stage — Part II, Articles 5–11

The citizenship provisions sit in Part II of the Constitution, written between Article 5 and Article 11. India follows Single Citizenship — unlike countries with dual (federal + state) citizenship — and this idea was borrowed from the UK Constitution.

🧠 Fig 1: Citizenship Provisions — Mind Map

Article 5 — Citizenship at the Commencement of the Constitution

Article 5 decides who was a citizen when the Constitution came into force, i.e. on 26 January 1950. Any ONE of these three conditions had to be true:

ConditionWhat It Means
BirthYou were born in India
ParentageEither parent (mother or father) was born in India
ResidenceYou had been an ordinary resident in India for the last 5 years before 26 January 1950
🎯 Exam Tip Article 5's three conditions are joined by "OR", not "AND" — satisfying any one of birth / parentage / 5-years' residence was enough to be a citizen at commencement.
❌ Common Mistake Don't assume you needed to satisfy all three conditions under Article 5 — meeting even one condition (say, just being born in India) was sufficient.
📖 Why do Articles 5–8 even exist? (tap to expand)
Articles 5, 6, 7 and 8 existed because of the huge influx and outflux of people around the time India became independent — people moving from Pakistan to India, and from India to Pakistan. These Articles were framed specifically for that transitional moment, which is why they don't carry much weight today.
🧠 Memory TrickArticle 5 = "5 conditions to check at the 1950 gate" — think of it as the entry gate to Indian citizenship: born here, OR parent born here, OR 5 years resident here.

Migration Provisions — Articles 6, 7 & 8

These three Articles were framed for people who moved across the newly-drawn India–Pakistan border around Independence. They no longer have practical relevance today — just remember the one-line theme of each, don't dig deeper.

ArticleSubject Matter
Article 6Citizenship of certain persons who migrated to India from Pakistan
Article 7Rights of citizenship of certain migrants to Pakistan (persons who went to Pakistan and then came back to India)
Article 8Rights of citizenship of certain persons of Indian origin residing outside India (in some other country — nothing to do with Pakistan)
🧠 Fig 2: How Articles 6–9 Connect

Article 9 — No Dual Citizenship

Articles 5, 6, 7, 8 don't exist as active/operative provisions today — they were relevant only for that transition period. Article 9 is the one still conceptually important: if a person voluntarily acquires citizenship of a foreign country, that person will not be a citizen of India.

📜 "There is no provision of dual citizenship in India."
❌ Common Mistake Don't confuse things like OCI cards with dual citizenship — the source rule under Article 9 is clear: India has no dual citizenship provision at all.
🎯 Exam Tip For Articles 5–8, examiners test only the surface-level line written in each Article — don't over-read into them, since they applied only around Independence-time migration.
💡 Quick Way to RememberArticles 6, 7, 8 are all about where people were physically going — Pakistan→India, India→Pakistan→India, or living in some other country altogether.

Articles 10–11 & The Move to the Citizenship Act

Article 10 — Continuance of the Rights of Citizenship

Once you become an Indian citizen, that citizenship continues. It isn't the case that some new law can be made overnight to suddenly debar you from citizenship.

Article 11 — Parliament's Exclusive Power

Article 11 says that only Parliament has the power to make laws regarding citizenship — not the State Legislature. This is because Citizenship is a subject in the Union List (List I of the Seventh Schedule).

🧠 Fig 3: Who Can Legislate on Citizenship?
❌ Common Mistake Don't think citizenship laws can be made at the state level in any circumstance — Article 11 hands this power exclusively to Parliament, because it's a Union List subject.
📖 Quick Recap: Why does the "List" matter here? (tap to expand)
Citizenship is placed under the Union List (List I) of the Seventh Schedule. Subjects in the Union List can be legislated on only by Parliament — this is exactly why Article 11 gives Parliament, and not any State Legislature, the power to frame citizenship law.

From the Constitution to the Citizenship Act

Articles 5 through 8 dealt with citizenship at Independence. The law that governs citizenship today is an Act of Parliament — the Citizenship Act — made using the very power granted by Article 11.

Before 26 Jan 1950
Articles 5–8 govern who is a citizen at the transition around Independence.
26 Jan 1950
Constitution commences — Article 5's conditions apply from this date.
1955
Parliament enacts the Citizenship Act under its Article 11 power — this is the law that operates today.
Later
The Citizenship (Amendment) Act, i.e. the CAA, amends this 1955 Act — details on Page 5.
🗓️ The Citizenship Act, 1955 = the law that governs citizenship rules TODAY!
🧠 Memory TrickArticles 5–8 = "the past" (Independence-time rules); the Citizenship Act, 1955 = "the present" (made possible by Article 11's Union-List power).

Acquiring Citizenship — The Citizenship Act, 1955

The Citizenship Act deals with the present-day rules. It lays down five ways in which a person can acquire Indian citizenship.

🧠 Fig 4: Five Ways to Acquire Citizenship
ModeKey Requirement
By BirthBeing born (as commonly understood under the Act)
By DescentPerson is of Indian origin but currently lives in another country
By RegistrationPerson of Indian origin holding another country's citizenship, or whose parents were once Indian citizens — can apply after 7 years of residence in India; also available if one's husband is an Indian citizen, again after 7 years' residence
By NaturalizationRequires living in India for approximately 12 years
By Incorporation of TerritoryApplies when India takes over a new territory — citizenship provisions are then framed for the people living there
Birth→ Descent→ Registration (7 yrs)→ Naturalization (12 yrs)→ Incorporation of Territory
📖 Incorporation of Territory — a hypothetical example (tap to expand)
This mode applies if India were to acquire a new territory. As a purely hypothetical illustration: if, say, a part of Pakistan such as Balochistan were to become part of India, the Act's incorporation-of-territory provisions would decide the citizenship status of the people living there. This is just an example to understand the concept, not a historical event.
❌ Common Mistake Don't mix up the residence periods — Registration needs 7 years' residence, while Naturalization needs roughly 12 years. They are two separate routes with different timelines.
🎯 Exam Tip Remember the 5 modes with the acronym B-D-R-N-I: Birth, Descent, Registration, Naturalization, Incorporation of territory.
🧠 Memory TrickRegistration = "7 for the connected" (Indian origin/spouse route); Naturalization = "12 for the newcomer" (no prior Indian link needed).

Losing Citizenship & The CAA

Three Ways to Lose Citizenship

ModeMeaning
RenunciationYou voluntarily give up your Indian citizenship yourself
DeprivationYour Indian citizenship was obtained fraudulently
TerminationE.g., you took another country's citizenship without informing, or you were helping someone in another country — citizenship is then taken away
❌ Common Mistake Don't treat renunciation, deprivation and termination as the same thing — renunciation is voluntary, deprivation is about fraud in obtaining citizenship, and termination follows from actions like secretly taking foreign citizenship or aiding another country.

The Citizenship (Amendment) Act — CAA

The CAA amends the Citizenship Act of 1955. It covers six communities — Hindus, Sikhs, Buddhists, Jains, Parsis, and Christians — coming from three countries: Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Afghanistan.

  • These six communities from these three countries are exempted from the normal rules of citizenship by naturalization
  • Instead of the usual 12 years, they only need to live in India for six years
  • This is the reason for the controversy around the CAA — it excludes the Muslim community
🧠 Fig 5: CAA — The 6-Year Fast-Track
📜 "This is the reason for the controversy, as it excludes the Muslim community."
📖 What exactly does the CAA change? (tap to expand)
The CAA doesn't create a brand-new citizenship route — it only changes the naturalization timeline for the named six communities from the named three countries, cutting the required residence period from 12 years down to 6 years.
🧠 Memory TrickCAA in three numbers: 6 communities, 3 countries, 6 years (down from 12).

⭐ Exam-Ready Summary

  • Citizenship provisions: Part II, Articles 5–11
  • Type of citizenship: Single citizenship (from the UK Constitution)
  • Article 5: birth OR parent born in India OR 5 years' residence before 26 Jan 1950
  • Articles 6–8: migration-related provisions (Pakistan ↔ India, Indian-origin abroad) — historical, at commencement only
  • Article 9: no dual citizenship
  • Article 10: continuance of citizenship rights
  • Article 11: only Parliament can legislate on citizenship (Union List, List I)
  • Citizenship Act, 1955: Birth, Descent, Registration (7 yrs), Naturalization (~12 yrs), Incorporation of Territory
  • Loss of citizenship: Renunciation, Deprivation, Termination
  • CAA: 6 communities, 3 countries, naturalization cut from 12 to 6 years — controversial for excluding Muslims
🎯 Exam Tip If asked "what does the CAA do?" — just recall three numbers: 6 communities, 3 countries, 6 years (instead of 12).
