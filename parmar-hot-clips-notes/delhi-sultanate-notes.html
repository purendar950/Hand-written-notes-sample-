Setting the Stage — The Delhi Sultanate

Five dynasties ruled the Delhi Sultanate, one after another, from 1206 AD to 1526 AD: Slave (Mamluk), then Khilji, then Tughlaq, then Sayyid, then Lodi.

DynastyTime Period
Slave / Mamluk1206 – 1290 AD
Khilji1290 – 1320 AD
Tughlaq1320 – 1414 AD
Sayyid1414 – 1451 AD
Lodi1451 – 1526 AD
Slave 1206→ Khilji 1290→ Tughlaq 1320→ Sayyid 1414→ Lodi 1451–1526
🧠 Memory Trick Remember the order using the first letters: S-K-T-S-L — Slave, Khilji, Tughlaq, Sayyid, Lodi. Say it out loud a few times like a chant before the exam!

Why "Slave Dynasty"?

It is called the Slave (Mamluk) Dynasty because its important rulers were slaves of one another. Most belonged to the Ilbari tribe — for example, Qutbuddin Aibak himself belonged to this tribe.

1️⃣ Qutbuddin Aibak (1206 – 1210 AD)

  • He was a slave of Muhammad Ghori
  • Ruled for four years, from 1206 to 1210 AD
  • Capital: Lahore
  • Built Adhai Din Ka Jhonpra (in Ajmer) and the Quwwat-ul-Islam mosque
  • Quwwat-ul-Islam was built using the corbel arch technique
  • Started construction of the Qutub Minar, in memory of his guru, the Sufi saint Khwaja Qutbuddin Bakhtiyar Kaki
  • Title: Lakh Baksh (Giver of Lakhs) — because he donated lakhs in charity
  • Died while playing polo (a horseback game)
🏗️ Fig 1: The Qutub Minar — Who Built What?
❌ Common Mistake Don't say "Qutbuddin Aibak built the Qutub Minar" — he only started its construction. It was completed by Iltutmish (5 storeys total), and the top storey was rebuilt by Firoz Shah Tughlaq after it was damaged by lightning.
📖 Adhai Din Ka Jhonpra — quick note (tap to expand)
The Adhai Din Ka Jhonpra is located in Ajmer. Along with the Quwwat-ul-Islam mosque in Delhi, it shows Qutbuddin Aibak's early building activity, both using the corbel arch technique typical of this transitional period.
🧠 Memory Trick"Lakh Baksh" = Lakhs + Baksh (to give) → the sultan who gave away lakhs in charity. Link the title directly to the meaning, don't just memorise the word!

Iltutmish & Razia Sultan

2️⃣ Iltutmish (1210 – 1236 AD)

Iltutmish was a slave of Qutbuddin Aibak, just as Aibak had been a slave of Muhammad Ghori.

🔗 Fig 2: The Slave Succession Chain
  • Shifted the capital from Lahore to Delhi — because Qutbuddin Aibak's son was based in Lahore
  • Introduced coins: the silver Tanka and the copper Jital
  • Introduced the Chahalgani system — a group of 40 Turkish loyal slaves given command of the entire administration
  • Introduced the Iqta system
  • His vizier (Prime Minister) was Nizam-ul-Mulk
💡 Understand the Iqta system like this... A military commander was given a piece of land (an Iqta). In return, he had to maintain troops and support the Sultan in battles. What did he get? The right to collect revenue from that land. The holder was called a Muqti / Wali.
🧠 Memory Trick Chahalgani = "Chahal" sounds like 40 in Persian → Group of 40 Turkish slaves controlling the whole administration for Iltutmish.

During his reign, Genghis Khan invaded the region around India while chasing a man — Iltutmish refused to give that man shelter, and so Genghis Khan did not attack India directly.

3️⃣ Razia Sultan (1236 – 1240 AD)

  • She was the daughter of Iltutmish — not a slave
  • She was the first female Islamic ruler in medieval history, and the only woman ruler of the Delhi Sultanate
  • Ruled for only four years
📜 In his book Tabaqat-i-Nasiri, the chronicler Minhaj-i-Siraj described Razia Sultan as more capable than even Iltutmish's own sons.
👤 Razia Sultan's downfall — full story (tap to expand)
Razia took Yaqut, an Ethiopian slave, into her close confidence. This upset the Chahalgani — she was a woman, and Yaqut was Ethiopian and outside their group. Altunia, governor of Bhatinda, refused to accept her authority. Yaqut was killed by Altunia, and Razia was imprisoned. She then married Altunia, and both marched towards Delhi to reclaim the throne, but were defeated — believed to be due to the Khokhar tribe. They were imprisoned in the Fort of Mubarak, Bhatinda, and died in the region near Haryana in 1240 AD.
❌ Common Mistake Don't call Razia Sultan a "slave ruler" — Aibak and Iltutmish were slaves, but Razia was Iltutmish's daughter, not a slave.
🎯 Exam Tip Iltutmish's 3 pillars: Tanka (silver) + Jital (copper) + Chahalgani (40 slaves) + Iqta system — and he shifted the capital from Lahore to Delhi. Learn these together as one block.

From Balban to the Rise of the Khiljis

Between 1240 and 1266 AD, weak puppet rulers were installed by the Chahalgani whenever it suited them — for example Muizuddin Bahram Shah, a son of Iltutmish.

4️⃣ Balban (1266 – 1287 AD)

  • He himself was a slave of Iltutmish and part of the Chahalgani
  • Before becoming Sultan, he was the Naib (chief minister) under the earlier ruler, Nasiruddin Mahmud
  • Broke the power of the Chahalgani to secure his own authority
  • Declared himself Zill-i-Ilahi — "Shadow of God"
  • Introduced the customs of Sijda and Paibos — subjects had to prostrate and kiss the monarch's feet
  • Claimed descent from the royal family of Afrasiyab
  • Followed the "Blood and Iron" policy — e.g. crushing the rebellion of Tughril Beg, the Governor of Bengal
  • Title: Ulugh Khan
  • Started the Persian festival Navroz
🎯 Exam Tip Balban's four "power" ideas go together: broke the Chahalgani → Zill-i-Ilahi → Sijda-Paibos → Blood and Iron policy — all about strengthening the throne.

Balban's grandson Kaiqubad became the last ruler of the Slave Dynasty (1287–1290 AD). He was killed by Jalaluddin Khilji, who then founded the Khilji Dynasty.

5️⃣ Khilji Dynasty Begins (1290 – 1320 AD)

  • Jalaluddin Khilji (1290–1296 AD) was the first ruler — nothing else major is studied about him
  • He was killed by his own nephew (who was also married to his daughter) — Alauddin Khilji

6️⃣ Alauddin Khilji (1296 – 1316 AD)

  • Real name: Ali Gurshasp
  • First expedition: Gujarat, 1298 AD — he looted it, and here he found the slave Malik Kafur, bought for 1000 dinars (hence also called "Hazar Dinari")
  • Expedition to Ranthambore, and to Mewar (1303 AD) — king of Mewar was Raja Ratan Singh, capital Chittor
  • Also expeditions to Malwa and Jalore
🔥 Jauhar — where Rajput women immolated themselves rather than be taken into the Haram (Islamic royal household). The first Jauhar happened at Mewar/Chittor.
📖 Padmavat — backstory (tap to expand)
The story of Raja Ratan Singh of Mewar, his capital Chittor, and Alauddin Khilji's siege is the subject of the epic poem Padmavat, written by Malik Muhammad Jayasi.

Malik Kafur's Deccan Campaigns

Malik Kafur led all of Alauddin's expeditions into South India, plundering (not yet conquering/ruling) the Deccan kingdoms one by one.

Alauddin Khilji — Reforms, Titles & Legacy

Market Reforms

Alauddin paid his soldiers low salaries but kept them happy by strictly controlling market prices.

  • Three markets were set up: Grain market, Cloth market (called Sarai Adil), and the Slave market
  • Each market was controlled by an officer called a Shahna
  • Spies called Barid and Munhiyan checked whether goods were being sold at fixed prices
🏪 Fig 4: Alauddin's Market Control System
❌ Common Mistake Don't mix up the roles: the Shahna was the officer who ran/regulated the market itself, while Barid & Munhiyan were spies who secretly checked whether the fixed prices were actually being followed.

Taxes

  • Kharaj — land tax paid by farmers
  • Ghari — house tax
  • Charai — pastoral (grazing) tax
🧠 Memory TrickGhari–Charai–Kharaj = House–Pasture–Land. Three taxes, three types of property!

Daag & Chehra System

Daag = branding of horses (a stamp, to prevent double counting). Chehra = the Huliya system — a descriptive roll of each soldier's appearance.

Titles & Court

  • Title Sikandar-i-Sani ("Second Alexander") given by his court poet Amir Khusrau
  • Amir Khusrau himself was titled Tuti-i-Hind ("Parrot of India")
  • Amir Khusrau wrote Nuh Sipihr and Tughlaq Nama, and is credited with inventing the sitar
  • Established the department Diwan-e-Mustakhraj — for clearing pending/arrear payments

Buildings

  • Alai Fort and Alai Darwaza (entrance gateway to the Qutub Minar complex)
  • Hauz Khas
  • Founded the city of Siri with Siri Fort — the second city of Delhi
🎯 Exam Tip Alauddin Khilji also abolished the Iqta system that Iltutmish had introduced — remember this "abolish" fact carefully, since it gets reversed later by Firoz Shah Tughlaq!
👑 The messy succession after Alauddin (tap to expand)
Alauddin had two sons: Mubarak Shah and Shahabuddin Umar. On Alauddin's death, Malik Kafur installed the younger son, Shahabuddin Umar. Mubarak Shah then killed his younger brother and took the throne, aided by Khusro Khan — much like Malik Kafur had once aided Alauddin. Khusro Khan then killed Mubarak Shah himself, but was in turn killed by Ghiyasuddin Tughlaq, who founded the Tughlaq Dynasty.

The Tughlaq Dynasty (1320 – 1414 AD)

7️⃣ Ghiyasuddin Tughlaq (1320 – 1325 AD)

Built the city of Tughlaqabad and the Tughlaqabad Fort.

8️⃣ Muhammad bin Tughlaq — the "Wisest Fool"

Known as Prince Jauna or Ulugh Khan before becoming Sultan. Called the "Wisest Fool" because his ideas were clever in theory but failed badly in execution.

📉 Fig 5: Three Bold Ideas — All Failed!
  • Shifted the capital from Delhi to Daulatabad (Devagiri, earlier ruled by the Yadavas) — to help conquer South India, then reversed the decision when it failed
  • Introduced a token currency, which also failed
  • His Khorasan and Qarachil expeditions (towards Iran/Himalayan region) failed; disbanding the army raised for it led to looting
  • Per chronicler Ziauddin Barani's Tarikh-i-Firozshahi, he appointed a gardener (Ladha and Peera), a barber (Firoz Hajjam), and a wine distiller (Aziz Khumar) to high administrative posts
  • Established the department Diwan-e-Kohi — the Department of Agriculture
🧠 Memory TrickGardner's names: Ladha & Peera — an easy exam-favourite pair to remember by sound.

During his time, the traveller Ibn Battuta, from Morocco, visited India and later wrote the travelogue Rihla.

9️⃣ Firoz Shah Tughlaq (1351 – 1388 AD)

He worked to clean up the mess left behind by Muhammad bin Tughlaq.

  • Developed many canals — a proper irrigation system for agriculture
  • Separated the Jaziya as its own distinct tax
  • Made the Iqta system hereditary (remember: Alauddin Khilji had earlier abolished it)
  • Founded Jaunpur (named after Muhammad bin Tughlaq, i.e. "Jauna," his cousin) and Firozabad (named after himself)
  • Established two departments: Diwan-e-Bandagan (Department of Slaves) and Diwan-e-Khairat (Department of Donations)
  • Built the hospital Darul Shifa
  • Rebuilt the top storey of the Qutub Minar after lightning damage
🎯 Exam Tip Iqta system timeline: Introduced by Iltutmish → Abolished by Alauddin Khilji → Made hereditary by Firoz Shah Tughlaq. This exact sequence is a favourite exam question!

Later, during the reign of Mahmud Shah Tughlaq, Timur launched repeated invasions that completely destroyed the Tughlaq Dynasty. Timur's own military commander, Khizr Khan, then laid the foundation of the Sayyid Dynasty.

🧠 Memory Trick "Wisest Fool" chain: Daulatabad shift → Token currency → Khorasan/Qarachil expeditions — three bold, clever-sounding plans, three total failures.

Sayyid & Lodi Dynasties — End of the Sultanate

🔟 Sayyid Dynasty (1414 – 1451 AD)

  • Founder: Khizr Khan
  • Members were known as Kulah-daran, because they wore a distinctive cone-shaped cap
  • Chronicler Yahya bin Ahmad Sirhindi wrote Tarikh-i-Mubarak Shahi, describing the Sayyids as descendants of the Prophet Muhammad
❌ Common Mistake Every court chronicler tended to flatter the ruling dynasty of their time — remember that Yahya bin Ahmad Sirhindi's claim about the Sayyids' descent comes from a court chronicle, not an independent historical verification.

1️⃣1️⃣ Lodi Dynasty (1451 – 1526 AD) — The Last Dynasty

  • Founder: Bahlol Lodi (1451–1489 AD), of Afghan origin
  • Sikandar Lodi (1489–1517 AD):
    • Composed poems under the pen name Gulrukhi
    • Built the city of Agra (1504 AD) and made it his capital in 1506 AD
    • Introduced a unit of measurement, Gaj-i-Sikandari (equal to 32 digits)
  • The Lodi Dynasty is known for introducing double-dome architecture in India
  • Ibrahim Lodi (1517–1526 AD) — the last ruler of the entire Delhi Sultanate
🧠 Memory TrickSikandar Lodi = Agra (city) + Gulrukhi (pen name) + Gaj-i-Sikandari (measurement) — three "his-own-name" contributions!

The First Battle of Panipat (1526 AD)

Fought between Ibrahim Lodi and Babur — Babur won, and Ibrahim Lodi was defeated.

⚔️ Fig 6: The First Battle of Panipat, 1526
🗝️ Just as King Jaichand once invited Muhammad Ghori, Daulat Khan Lodi invited Babur to defeat Ibrahim Lodi — hoping to gain the throne himself. But just as Ghori had done with Jaichand, Babur did the same to Daulat Khan Lodi — and took the throne for himself instead.
🎯 Exam Tip 1526 = double milestone: it marks the end of the Delhi Sultanate (last ruler: Ibrahim Lodi) AND the start of the Mughal Dynasty (Babur) — in the very same battle!

⭐ Exam-Ready Summary — Delhi Sultanate

Slave 1206–1290→ Khilji 1290–1320→ Tughlaq 1320–1414→ Sayyid 1414–1451→ Lodi 1451–1526
  • Started: Qutbuddin Aibak, 1206 AD
  • Most powerful ruler: Alauddin Khilji (1296–1316 AD)
  • "Wisest Fool": Muhammad bin Tughlaq
  • Ended: Ibrahim Lodi, defeated at the First Battle of Panipat, 1526 AD
  • Result: End of the Delhi Sultanate → start of the Mughal Dynasty (Babur)
