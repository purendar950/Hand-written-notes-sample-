Babur — Founder of the Mughal Dynasty (1526 – 1530)

Babur was a bright, studious boy from childhood. His original name was Zahiruddin Muhammad Babur, and he used the title Padshah.

  • At the age of 12, he sat on the throne of Farghana (a region near Afghanistan) — but his own family members drove him out
  • He arrived and settled in Kabul in 1504, from where he planned his attack on India

The Tulughma Formation

In the First Battle of Panipat (1526), Babur used gunpowder on a large scale for the first time, along with his signature Tulughma formation.

⚔️ Fig 1: The Tulughma Formation
📖 How the Tulughma formation worked (tap to expand)
Babur's forces attacked from both flanks first, pushing the enemy towards hills on the sides so they could not run around and escape. The enemy was then forced to charge straight into the centre, where they were attacked from three sides at once. The right flank was led by Babur's eldest son, Humayun.

Writings & Death

  • Babur ruled from 1526 and died in 1530
  • His autobiography, Tuzuk-i-Babari, was originally written in Turkish
  • It was later translated into Persian as the Baburnama by Abdul Rahim Khan-i-Khana — one of Akbar's Navratnas (nine gems)
  • Babur was first buried in Agra, and later re-buried in Kabul, Afghanistan
❌ Common Mistake Don't say the Tuzuk-i-Babari was written in Persian — it was written in Turkish. The Baburnama is its Persian translation, done later by Abdul Rahim Khan-i-Khana.
🧠 Memory TrickBabur's burial: Agra first, then Kabul. "A" before "K" — Agra before Kabul, just like the alphabet!

Humayun & the Sher Shah Suri Interlude

Humayun's First Reign (1530 – 1540)

Before becoming Sultan, Humayun managed the Kandahar / Kabul region. His brothers were less capable rulers.

Humayun vs Sher Shah Suri

BattleYearWinner
Battle of Chausa1539 ADSher Shah Suri
Battle of Kannauj / Bilgram1540 ADSher Shah Suri

Defeated and chased, Humayun fled and took refuge with the Safavid Dynasty in Iran.

❌ Common Mistake Don't confuse the First Battle of Panipat (1526, Babur vs Ibrahim Lodi) with the Second Battle of Panipat (1556, Bairam Khan/Akbar vs Hemu) — thirty years and a whole Sur interlude apart!

Sher Shah Suri (r. 1540 – 1545)

  • Original name: Farid Khan; his father Hasan Khan was a Jagirdar in Sasaram, Bihar
  • His administration was so strong that it was later adopted by Akbar
  • Introduced the currency called the Rupee
  • Built the GT (Grand Trunk) Road — from Chittagong (present-day Bangladesh) to Kabul (also recorded as up to Peshawar)
  • Built Sarais (resting places) along the GT Road
  • Issued Patta and Qabuliyat — agreements between the state and peasants fixing land revenue
🛣️ Fig 2: Sher Shah's GT Road
💡 Understand it like this... Patta & Qabuliyat fixed exactly how much land revenue a peasant owed. This same idea of a fixed, assessed revenue later inspired Akbar's Dahsala (10-year) system under Todar Mal.

Sher Shah's Death (1545)

While attacking the strong walls of Kalinjar fort in 1545, a bomb he was using to break the wall exploded near his own feet. He remained alive for a couple of days before dying. He is buried in Sasaram, Bihar.

🎯 If this accident hadn't happened at Kalinjar, the Mughal dynasty might not have continued in India at all — it could have ended with Humayun!

Humayun Returns & the Second Battle of Panipat

  • Humayun's biography, the Humayun Nama, was written by his sister Gulbadan Begum
  • Humayun's Tomb in Delhi was commissioned by his wife Haji Begum (Bega Begum)
  • Humayun regained the throne in 1555 but died in 1556, from a fall down the library stairs — buried in Delhi
  • 13-year-old Akbar succeeded him, with Bairam Khan as regent
  • Since Sher Shah's Afghans were also weakened, a fresh contest followed: Bairam Khan (for Akbar) vs Hemu (Hemchandra Vikramaditya) — the Second Battle of Panipat, 1556
  • Akbar's side won; he was crowned at age 13 at Kalanaur, Punjab
🎯 Exam Tip Humayun's reign has two parts: 1530–1540 (before exile) and 1555–1556 (after regaining the throne). In between, it was the Sur Dynasty of Sher Shah Suri.

Akbar (1556 – 1605) — Rajputs, Revenue & Ranks

Akbar's original name was Jalaluddin Muhammad Akbar.

Rajput Policy

  • Akbar brought most Rajputs under control through marriage alliances or by giving them high administrative posts
  • He married Harka Bai (later known as Jodha Bai), daughter of Bharmal, the Kachhwaha Rajput ruler of Amer
  • Harka Bai was given the title Maryam-uz-Zamani; her son was Jahangir
  • One Rajput ruler refused to accept Akbar's suzerainty: Rana Sanga of the Sisodia clan (whom Babur had earlier defeated at Khanwa)
❌ Common Mistake The Battle of Haldighati (1576) was fought against Maharana Pratap, grandson of Rana Sanga — not against Akbar personally. Akbar's forces were led by Man Singh; the result was not a decisive victory for either side, and historians hold differing views.

Maharana Pratap never surrendered — he kept retreating and living in hiding while rebuilding his forces, effectively escaping Akbar's forces without a clear decisive outcome at Haldighati.

Land Revenue: The Dahsala (Zabti) System

Introduced by Akbar's finance minister and Navratna, Todar Mal. Land revenue was assessed on a 10-year average basis.

Land TypeDescription
PolajNever left fallow — most fertile
ParautiLeft fallow for 1–2 years
ChacharLeft fallow for 3–4 years
BanjarCompletely barren — no cultivation possible
🧠 Memory Trick Polaj → Parauti → Chachar → Banjar = fertile going down to barren, in that exact order.

The Mansabdari System (1571)

Mansab means rank. Ranks were allotted based on how much military force a noble maintained.

🎖️ Fig 3: How the Mansabdari System Worked
  • Zat = infantry maintained; Sawar = horsemen/cavalry maintained
  • Based on this, a noble was either paid a salary or given full revenue rights (a jagir) — making him both a mansabdar and a jagirdar
  • Ranks ranged from 10 up to 7000 during Akbar's reign (later up to 10,000 under Aurangzeb)
  • The top rank of 7000 was normally reserved for Mughal princes
  • Raja Man Singh was given a mansab of 7000 — he was called Saptahazari
🎯 Exam Tip Mansabdari (1571) is very close in date to Fatehpur Sikri (1570) — examiners often pair these two years together!

Akbar — Religion, Learning & Architecture

Din-i-Ilahi

Din-i-Ilahi was a new religion — a blend of the best elements from all religions. Birbal was the only courtier who accepted it; overall, the religion never gained wide popularity.

❌ Common Mistake Din-i-Ilahi was not a popular or widely-adopted faith — remember that only Birbal among Akbar's courtiers accepted it.

Ibadat Khana & Maktab Khana

  • Ibadat Khana — the hall where religious discourses/discussions took place
  • Maktab Khana — the translation department
  • Ramayana translated into Persian by Abdul Qadir Badauni
  • Mahabharata translated into Persian by Badauni and Faizi, titled Razmnama
🎯 Exam Tip Don't mix up Ibadat Khana (religious discussion hall) with Maktab Khana (translation department) — different buildings, different purposes!

Akbarnama & Ain-i-Akbari

Written by Abul Fazl (a Navratna), the Akbarnama has three volumes:

  1. Akbar's ancestors
  2. Akbar's reign / tenure
  3. Akbar's administration — known separately as the Ain-i-Akbari

Architecture

  • Built Fatehpur Sikri in 1570, in memory of Sheikh Salim Chishti, who blessed Akbar with a son (Jahangir)
  • Built the Buland Darwaza at Fatehpur Sikri in 1575, to commemorate his victory in Gujarat
  • Built the Panch Mahal (at Fatehpur Sikri) and the Agra Fort
  • Capital shifts: Agra → Fatehpur Sikri (1571) → Lahore (1585) → back to Agra (1598)
🧠 Memory Trick"Fat Man" — Fatehpur Sikri built in 1570, Mansabdari system introduced in 1571. Two big Akbar events, one year apart!
💎 Fig 4: Akbar's Navratnas (Nine Gems)
👤 Tansen — quick backstory (tap to expand)
Tansen's original name was Ramtanu Pandey. He served in the court of Raja Ramchandra Singh of Gwalior before Akbar summoned him. Akbar gave him the title Miyan Tansen. His own guru was Swami Haridas.

Akbar's last military expedition was to Khandesh (part of Ahmednagar, South India) in 1601, which he captured — but he had to turn back when Jahangir revolted against him. Akbar died and was buried in Agra.

Jahangir (1605 – 1627)

Jahangir's original name was Nuruddin Muhammad Salim, also known as Prince Salim.

👑 Fig 5: Jahangir's Family & Succession
  • Jahangir struggled with addiction, and did little beyond his marriages
  • His wife Manmati Bai, also known as Jagat Gosain, was the mother of Shah Jahan
  • He later married Mehrunnisa — after defeating an Afghan in Bihar — who was given the title Noor Jahan
📌 Real Power Because of Jahangir's addiction, actual administrative control fell largely into Noor Jahan's hands.

Noor Jahan's Buildings (in Lahore)

  • Tomb of Itmad-ud-Daula (built in memory of her father)
  • Noor Mahal

Jahangir himself is credited with building the Moti Masjid in Lahore (one of three Moti Masjids in Mughal architecture — see Shah Jahan's page).

❌ Common Mistake Shah Jahan was not born to Noor Jahan — his mother was Jahangir's earlier wife, Manmati Bai (Jagat Gosain).
🧠 Memory Trick Jahangir's birth name had "Salim" in it — think "Prince Salim" as his personal/pre-throne identity, just like Akbar was "Jalaluddin" before his title.

Shah Jahan (1628 – 1658)

Shah Jahan's original name was Prince Khurram, born to Manmati Bai (not Noor Jahan).

  • Mahabat Khan, Jahangir's military commander, along with Shah Jahan, extended Mughal control in South India, capturing Ahmednagar and other territories
  • Mahabat Khan grew irritated with Noor Jahan's influence and revolted against Jahangir
  • After Jahangir's death, Shah Jahan and Mahabat Khan together removed Noor Jahan from power

The Taj Mahal

Shah Jahan's wife, Mumtaz Mahal, died in 1631, just three years into his reign. He built the Taj Mahal in Agra in her memory.

🕌 Fig 6: The Taj Mahal — Key Facts
❌ Common Mistake Don't confuse Ustad Ahmad Lahori (the architect who built the Taj Mahal) with Abdul Hamid Lahori (who wrote the Badshahnama, Shah Jahan's official biography) — very similar names, very different jobs!

Other Buildings & Features

  • Moti Masjid, Agra — one of three Moti Masjids: Lahore (Jahangir), Agra (Shah Jahan), Delhi (Aurangzeb)
  • Diwan-i-Aam (Hall of Public Audience) and Diwan-i-Khas (Hall of Private Audience) in his forts
  • The Peacock Throne, embedded with the Kohinoor diamond, used over 1,000 kg of gold
  • Sheesh Mahal — a palace decorated with glass/mirror work
  • Pietra Dura — an inlay-art style using precious/semi-precious stones, seen at the Taj Mahal
🧠 Memory TrickBernier wrote "Travels in the Mogul Empire"; Tavernier wrote "Travels in India" — both French travellers at Shah Jahan's court.

War of Succession (1658)

Shah Jahan wanted his eldest son, Dara Shikoh, to succeed him. But Dara Shikoh, being of a gentler temperament, was defeated by his brother Aurangzeb.

⚔️ The succession battles (tap to expand)
Three battles were fought in 1658: the Battle of Dharmat, the Battle of Samugarh, and the Battle of Deorai. Aurangzeb won all three, had Dara Shikoh killed, and imprisoned his own father, Shah Jahan, before beginning his own rule.

Aurangzeb (1658 – 1707)

Aurangzeb took the title Alamgir, and was also given the title Zinda Pir ("Living Saint").

Religious Policy

  • A devout Muslim, he appointed Muhtasibs — officials who checked whether people were following Islamic religious codes
  • Issued the Fatwa-i-Alamgiri, a compilation of Islamic laws for all to follow
  • Forced religious conversions, including on Kashmiri Pandits, who resisted
  • When Guru Tegh Bahadur Ji refused to convert and support forced conversion, Aurangzeb had him executed
❌ Common Mistake Alamgir and Zinda Pir are two separate titles of Aurangzeb — don't treat them as the same word or mix up which one means "world-seizer" (Alamgir) versus "living saint" (Zinda Pir).

Completing the Deccan Conquest

With Ahmednagar, Berar, and Bidar already captured earlier, Aurangzeb captured Bijapur in 1686 and Golconda in 1687.

Conflict with the Marathas

⚔️ Fig 7: Aurangzeb vs the Marathas — Timeline
🧠 Memory TrickMarathas' two revenue tools: Chauth (1/4th of revenue) + Sardeshmukhi (extra levy) — guerrilla raids funded by both.
📖 Treaty of Purandar & Shivaji's escape (tap to expand)
Aurangzeb sent Raja Jai Singh to negotiate with Shivaji, resulting in the Treaty of Purandar (1665), under which Shivaji surrendered 23 of his 35 forts. Aurangzeb later captured Shivaji through deceit, but Shivaji escaped without Aurangzeb even realising how. In 1674, Shivaji crowned himself Chhatrapati at the fort of Raigad, declaring independence from Mughal suzerainty.

Shivaji's son Sambhaji also refused to accept Mughal suzerainty; when eventually captured, Aurangzeb had him put to death in a brutal manner.

Decline & Death

  • Aurangzeb spent his last 20+ years camped in the Deccan (Aurangabad, today's Sambhaji Nagar), never returning to Delhi — this marks the beginning of Mughal decline
  • Died in 1707, buried in Aurangabad (Sambhaji Nagar)
  • Built the Moti Masjid in Delhi
  • His son Azam Shah built Bibi Ka Maqbara (called the "Mini Taj Mahal") in memory of his mother, Dilras Banu Begum (also known as Rabia Durrani)
🎯 Exam Tip Aurangzeb himself admitted his biggest mistake was leaving Delhi for the Deccan — the Marathas never fully submitted, and the empire weakened at its core while he was away.

The Later Mughals & Decline

📉 Fig 8: Later Mughal Succession
  • Bahadur Shah I (1707–1712), also known as Shah Alam I, ascended the throne with the help of Zulfiqar Khan
  • Jahandar Shah ruled for just one year (1712–1713)
  • Farrukhsiyar (1713–1719) ascended with the help of the Sayyid Brothers (Hussain Ali Khan Barha & Abdullah Khan Barha) — known as "Kingmakers"
  • Farrukhsiyar issued the Golden Farman to the British in 1717 (they had cured him of an illness)
  • The Sayyid Brothers later had Farrukhsiyar killed
❌ Common Mistake The Sayyid Brothers made Farrukhsiyar king and later killed him — don't assume they stayed in power forever. Muhammad Shah had them killed early in his own reign, before they could do the same to him.

Muhammad Shah "Rangeela" (1719 – 1748)

  • His first major act was having the Sayyid Brothers killed
  • Known as "Rangeela" for his colourful, pleasure-loving personality — more interested in enjoyment than governance
  • During his reign, Nadir Shah invaded and plundered Delhi in 1739, carrying off the Peacock Throne along with the Kohinoor diamond embedded in it
🧠 Memory Trick"Rangeela" = colourful/pleasure-loving → neglect of administration → Nadir Shah's 1739 invasion happened right under his watch.

Mughal Architecture — One Last Note

The Char Bagh style of garden architecture (a four-quadrant garden layout) was developed by the Mughals — seen, for example, at Humayun's Tomb.

🎯 Exam Tip Bibi Ka Maqbara ("Mini Taj Mahal," built by Azam Shah for his mother) is often confused with the Taj Mahal itself — remember it copies the Taj's design but is a separate, later monument.

⭐ Exam-Ready Summary — The Mughal Dynasty

Babur 1526→ Humayun→ Akbar→ Jahangir→ Shah Jahan→ Aurangzeb→ Decline (1707+)
  • Founded: Babur, First Battle of Panipat, 1526 AD
  • Peak/expansion: Akbar (Rajput alliances, Mansabdari, Dahsala) & Shah Jahan (architecture)
  • Religious extremism & overreach: Aurangzeb
  • Turning point of decline: Aurangzeb's prolonged Deccan campaign against the Marathas
  • Foreign humiliation: Nadir Shah's sack of Delhi, 1739, under Muhammad Shah "Rangeela"
