① Seventh Schedule & Centre–State Powers ⚖️
Q1. According to the Indian Constitution, which of the following pair of subject & list is correct? (1) Adoption & Succession → State List (2) Trade & Commerce → Concurrent List
A
Only 1
B
Only 2
C
Both 1 & 2
D
Neither 1 nor 2 ✔
✅ Answer: D — Both wrong (interchanged). Adoption & Succession → Concurrent List; Trade & Commerce → State List
7th Schedule — Three Lists
ListOriginalNowKey Subjects
Union List97100Defence, Atomic Energy, Foreign Affairs, Banking, Railways, Post & Telegraph, Airways, Foreign Trade, Currency & Coins
State List6661Agriculture, Police, Prison, Local Govt, Public Health, Land, Trade & Commerce, Animal Husbandry
Concurrent List4752Education, Forests, Trade Unions, Adulteration, Adoption & Succession
DOCTRINE OF REPUGNANCY
Both Parliament & State Legislature can make laws on Concurrent List.
If both clash → Parliament's law prevails
Exception: State law prevails if State Bill was reserved for President by Governor under Article 200
BORROWED FROM
Concurrent List → Australia
Joint Sitting (Art 108) → Australia
Residuary Powers with Centre → Canada
🧠 C for Centre = C for Canada

5 Conditions — Parliament can legislate on State List subjects:

  • Art 249 — Rajya Sabha empowers Parliament via special majority
  • Art 250 — During National Emergency
  • Art 252 — Two or more states request Parliament
  • Art 253 — To enforce international treaties
  • Art 356 — When President's Rule is imposed

British Acts — Timeline
1861 Portfolio system; ordinance power to Viceroy; decentralisation; restored powers of Bombay & Madras; 3 Indians nominated to Council
1892 First power to discuss the budget; strength of Council increased
1909 Morley–Minto: Separate electorates for Muslims · Satyendra Prasad Sinha = 1st Indian on Viceroy's Executive Council · Council 16 → 60 · supplementary questions & resolutions on budget
1919 Montagu–Chelmsford: Diarchy in provinces · bicameral legislature at centre · separate electorates extended to Sikhs & Anglo-Indian Christians
1935 Removed diarchy from provinces (provincial autonomy); introduced diarchy at the centre
🧠 1773 Act → Governor of Bengal became Governor-General of Bengal · 1833 Act → became Governor-General of India (centralisation) · 1861 → decentralisation again
